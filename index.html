📖 Module 1.1: Strength of Materials Basics ▼
☐ Lesson 1: Stress & Strain - The Basic Concept Current

📝 What You Will Learn:

  • Stress (σ): Force per unit area. When you pull or push something, internal resistance develops.
  • Strain (ε): Deformation per unit length. How much something stretches compared to its original size.
  • Hooke's Law: Stress is directly proportional to strain within elastic limit.
  • Young's Modulus (E): Material stiffness constant.

💡 Real-Life Example:

Imagine pulling a rubber band:

  • The force you apply = Stress (internal resistance)
  • How much it stretches = Strain
  • A thick rubber band needs more force = High E (stiff)
  • A thin rubber band stretches easily = Low E (flexible)

In Construction: A concrete column carrying building weight develops compressive stress. If stress exceeds concrete capacity, column fails!

🔬 Key Definitions:

  • Axial Stress: σ = P/A (Force ÷ Area) in N/mm² or MPa
  • Strain: ε = δ/L (Change in length ÷ Original length) - unitless
  • Elastic Limit: Point beyond which material won't return to original shape
  • Yield Point: Where permanent deformation starts (for steel)

🔢 FORMULAS TO REMEMBER ALWAYS:

1. Stress: σ = P / A

   (P = Force in Newtons, A = Area in mm²)

2. Strain: ε = δ / L

   (δ = Deformation in mm, L = Original length in mm)

3. Hooke's Law: σ = E × ε OR E = σ / ε

   (E = Young's Modulus in MPa or GPa)

4. Deformation: δ = PL / AE

   (Most useful formula! Memorize this!)

📌 THUMB RULES (Quick Memory):

  • ✦ Steel E ≈ 200 GPa (200,000 MPa) - Very stiff
  • ✦ Concrete E ≈ 25-30 GPa - Less stiff than steel
  • ✦ 1 MPa = 1 N/mm² (Same thing, different units!)
  • ✦ Concrete compressive strength f'c = 25-50 MPa typically
  • ✦ Steel yield strength fy = 250 MPa (Fe250) or 500 MPa (Fe500)
  • ✦ Stress increases when area decreases (thinner = more stress)

💡 KEY CONCEPTS (Exam Important):

  • ● Stress is INTERNAL resistance, not external force
  • ● Tensile stress (+) = Pulling apart (steel in tension zone)
  • ● Compressive stress (-) = Pushing together (concrete in compression)
  • ● Shear stress = Forces acting parallel to surface (like cutting with scissors)
  • ● Within elastic limit, material returns to original shape when load removed
  • ● Beyond yield point = PERMANENT DAMAGE (plastic deformation)
  • ● Factor of Safety = Ultimate stress / Working stress (usually 1.5 to 3)
☐ Lesson 2: Bending Moment & Shear Force Diagrams (SFD/BMD) Pending

📝 What You Will Learn:

  • Shear Force (V): Internal force that tries to slide one part of beam relative to other
  • Bending Moment (M): Internal moment that tries to bend the beam
  • SFD: Graph showing shear force along beam length
  • BMD: Graph showing bending moment along beam length

💡 Real-Life Example:

Imagine a wooden plank over two supports, you stand on middle:

  • Shear: At supports, plank wants to "shear off" vertically - that's shear force
  • Bending: Middle of plank bends downward - top fibers squeeze (compression), bottom fibers stretch (tension)
  • If plank breaks at bottom first = Tension failure (concrete weak in tension!)
  • If plank breaks at support by sliding down = Shear failure

🔬 Key Relationships (MEMORIZE!):

  • dV/dx = -w (Rate of change of shear = negative of load intensity)
  • dM/dx = V (Rate of change of moment = shear value)
  • Where V = 0, M is MAXIMUM or MINIMUM (critical point!)
  • Point load causes JUMP in SFD, kink in BMD
  • UDL causes SLOPED line in SFD, CURVED (parabolic) in BMD

🔢 FORMULAS TO REMEMBER ALWAYS:

Simply Supported Beam - UDL (w) over full span (L):

Max Shear (at supports): V = wL/2

Max Moment (at center): M = wL²/8


Simply Supported Beam - Point Load (P) at center:

Max Shear: V = P/2

Max Moment (at center): M = PL/4


Cantilever Beam - UDL (w):

Max Shear (at fixed end): V = wL

Max Moment (at fixed end): M = wL²/2


Cantilever Beam - Point Load (P) at free end:

Max Shear: V = P

Max Moment (at fixed end): M = PL

📌 THUMB RULES FOR QUICK DRAWING:

  • ✦ Start from LEFT, move RIGHT
  • ✦ Upward reaction = UP jump in SFD
  • ✦ Downward load = DOWN jump/slope in SFD
  • ✦ UDL = Straight slope in SFD
  • ✦ No load = Horizontal line in SFD
  • ✦ BMD slope = Value of SFD at that point
  • ✦ Positive moment sags downward (smile shape 😊)
  • ✦ Negative moment hogs upward (frown shape ☹️)
  • ✦ Max moment where SFD crosses zero

💡 KEY CONCEPTS (Interview Questions):

  • ● Shear force is maximum near SUPPORTS
  • ● Bending moment is maximum at MID-SPAN (for simply supported)
  • ● For cantilever, both max shear AND max moment are at FIXED END
  • ● Point of Contraflexure: Where moment changes sign (BMD crosses zero)
  • ● In continuous beams, hogging moment occurs over supports
  • ● Always find reactions FIRST before drawing SFD/BMD
  • ● Sum of upward forces = Sum of downward forces (equilibrium)
☐ Lesson 3: Bending Stresses in Beams Pending

📝 What You Will Learn:

  • Flexure Formula: How to calculate bending stress at any point in beam
  • Neutral Axis: Where stress is ZERO
  • Section Modulus (Z): Beam's resistance to bending
  • Moment of Inertia (I): Property related to cross-section shape

💡 Real-Life Example:

Why do we put steel at BOTTOM of beam?

  • When beam sags under load: TOP gets squeezed (compression), BOTTOM stretches (tension)
  • Concrete is WEAK in tension (only 10% of its compressive strength!)
  • Steel is STRONG in tension
  • So we put steel where tension occurs = BOTTOM of beam for sagging moment
  • This is why understanding bending stress distribution is CRITICAL!

🔢 FORMULAS TO REMEMBER ALWAYS:

FLEXURE FORMULA (MOST IMPORTANT!):

σ = My/I

OR equivalently: σ = M/Z where Z = I/y


Where:

σ = Bending stress (MPa)

M = Bending moment at section (Nmm or kNm)

y = Distance from Neutral Axis to point (mm)

I = Moment of Inertia about NA (mm⁴)

Z = Section Modulus (mm³) = I/y_max


MOMENT OF INERTIA FORMULAS:

Rectangle (b×H): I = bH³/12 | Z = bH²/6

Circle (D): I = πD⁴/64 | Z = πD³/32

T-beam: Calculate using parallel axis theorem

📌 THUMB RULES:

  • ✦ Max stress occurs at FARTHEST point from neutral axis (top or bottom fiber)
  • ✦ Stress at neutral axis = ZERO
  • ✦ Deeper beam = Higher I = Lower stress (more efficient!)
  • ✦ Wider flange in T-beam = Better for compression zone
  • ✦ For rectangular beam: effective depth d ≈ 0.9 × total depth H
  • ✦ Max tensile stress at bottom (sagging), max compression at top

💡 KEY CONCEPTS:

  • ● Neutral axis passes through centroid of cross-section
  • ● For homogeneous beam: NA at mid-depth (symmetric sections)
  • ● For RC beam: NA shifts based on steel amount and concrete cracked/uncracked
  • ● Stress diagram is LINEAR (triangular distribution)
  • ● Compression zone above NA, Tension zone below NA (for positive/sagging moment)
  • ● Section modulus Z tells us "strength" of beam shape against bending
  • ● Higher Z = Stronger beam (can resist more moment)
☐ Lesson 4: Shear Stresses in Beams Pending

📝 What You Will Learn:

  • Shear Stress Formula: τ = VQ/(Ib) - how shear distributes
  • Shear Stress Distribution: Parabolic in rectangular sections
  • Maximum Shear: Occurs at neutral axis (opposite of bending!)
  • Shear vs Bending: Different failure modes, different locations

💡 Why Shear Matters:

Real example: Beam near support fails differently than at mid-span

  • At MID-SPAN: Bending dominates → Bottom cracks (tension failure)
  • At SUPPORT: Shear dominates → Diagonal cracks at ~45° angle
  • These diagonal cracks are called "shear cracks" or "diagonal tension cracks"
  • Sudden and DANGEROUS - less warning than bending failure!
  • That's why we provide STIRRUPS (shear reinforcement) near supports

🔢 FORMULAS TO REMEMBER:

General Shear Formula:

τ = VQ/(Ib)


For RECTANGULAR SECTION (most common):

Max shear (at NA): τ_max = 1.5 × V/(bd) = 3V/(2A)

Average shear: τ_avg = V/A

Note: τ_max = 1.5 × τ_avg for rectangles!


Where:

V = Shear force at section

Q = First moment of area above/below point

I = Moment of inertia of entire section

b = Width of section at that level

📌 THUMB RULES:

  • ✦ Max shear stress at NEUTRAL AXIS (max bending at extreme fibers!)
  • ✦ Shear stress at top/bottom fibers = ZERO
  • ✦ Rectangular beam: τ_max = 1.5 × average shear
  • ✦ I-beam/T-beam: Most shear carried by WEB (not flanges)
  • ✦ Shear failure is SUDDEN and BRITTLE (dangerous!)
  • ✦ Stirrups spacing is closest NEAR SUPPORTS (high shear zone)
  • ✦ First stirrup usually placed at d/2 from face of support

💡 KEY CONCEPTS:

  • ● Shear stress distribution is PARABOLIC in rectangular beams
  • ● Diagonal tension causes inclined cracks at ~45°
  • ● Stirrups resist diagonal tension by providing "truss action"
  • ● Concrete alone can resist some shear (Vc) - rest taken by stirrups (Vs)
  • ● Total shear capacity: φVn = φ(Vc + Vs) ≥ Vu
  • ● Critical section for shear is at d distance from face of support
  • ● Minimum stirrups always required even if calculation shows no need!
📘 Module 1.2: Reinforced Concrete Design Basics (ACI 318) ▼
☐ Lesson 5: Introduction to RC Design & Limit State Method Pending

📝 What You Will Learn:

  • Limit State Method: Modern design philosophy used worldwide
  • Two Limit States: Serviceability (deflection, crack) & Ultimate (strength, collapse)
  • Safety Factors (φ): Reduction factors for different failure modes
  • Load Combinations: How to combine different loads safely

💡 Why Not Just Use Actual Loads?

Example: A beam designed for exactly 100 kN load

  • What if actual load becomes 105 kN? (material stored on floor)
  • What if concrete is slightly weaker than tested?
  • What if construction has minor defects?
  • Solution: Design for FACTORED loads (higher than actual) and reduce capacity by φ factor
  • This gives us SAFETY MARGIN without being overly conservative

🔢 FORMULAS TO REMEMBER ALWAYS:

LOAD COMBINATIONS (ACI 318):

U = 1.2D + 1.6L (Most common!)
U = 1.2D + 1.0W + L
U = 0.9D + 1.0W


STRENGTH REDUCTION FACTORS (φ):

Flexure (tension controlled): φ = 0.90
Shear and Torsion: φ = 0.75
Bond/Anchorage: φ = 0.75
Compression (spirals): φ = 0.75
Compression (ties): φ = 0.65


BASIC DESIGN EQUATION:

φMn ≥ Mu

(Design moment capacity must be ≥ Factored moment demand)

📌 THUMB RULES:

  • ✦ 1.2D + 1.6L is your DEFAULT combination for most designs
  • ✦ Live load factor (1.6) > Dead load factor (1.2) because live load is more uncertain
  • ✦ φ = 0.9 for flexure means we use 90% of calculated capacity
  • ✦ Lower φ for shear/compression = these failures are more dangerous/less predictable
  • ✦ Serviceability checks use ACTUAL (unfactored) loads
  • ✦ Ultimate strength checks use FACTORED loads

💡 KEY CONCEPTS:

  • ● Dead Load (D): Self-weight + permanent fixtures (calculate accurately)
  • ● Live Load (L): Occupants, furniture, movable items (use code values)
  • ● Service Limit State: Crack width limits, deflection limits, vibration
  • ● Ultimate Limit State: Collapse prevention, overturning, sliding
  • ● WSD (Working Stress Method) is OLD - ACI uses USD/LSD now
  • ● Balanced condition: Steel yields SAME TIME concrete crushes (boundary point)
  • ● Tension-controlled: Steel yields FIRST (ductile - desirable!)
  • ● Compression-controlled: Concrete crushes FIRST (brittle - avoid!)
☐ Lesson 6: Singly Reinforced Beam Design (Step-by-Step) Pending

📝 This Is THE MOST IMPORTANT LESSON in RC Design!

You will design a beam completely from scratch. Master this, and everything else builds on it.

💡 Complete Worked Example:

PROBLEM: Design a simply supported rectangular beam

  • Span = 6 m
  • Dead Load (excluding self-weight) = 15 kN/m
  • Live Load = 20 kN/m
  • f'c = 28 MPa, fy = 420 MPa
  • Assume b = 300 mm

STEP 1: Calculate Effective Depth (d)

For simply supported beam: d ≈ L/12 to L/10

d = 6000/12 = 500 mm (try d = 500 mm, total H ≈ 550 mm with cover)

STEP 2: Calculate Self-Weight

Unit weight of concrete = 25 kN/m³

Self-weight = 0.30 × 0.55 × 25 = 4.125 kN/m

Total DL = 15 + 4.125 = 19.125 kN/m

STEP 3: Factored Load (Wu)

Wu = 1.2D + 1.6L = 1.2(19.125) + 1.6(20) = 22.95 + 32 = 54.95 kN/m

STEP 4: Maximum Moment (Mu)

Mu = WuL²/8 = 54.95 × 6²/8 = 247.3 kNm = 247.3 × 10⁶ Nmm

STEP 5: Required Rn Value

Rn = Mu / (φbd²) = (247.3×10⁶) / (0.9 × 300 × 500²) = 3.66 MPa

STEP 6: Find Reinforcement Ratio (ρ)

Using: Rn = ρfy(1 - 0.5ρfy/0.85f'c)

Solving: ρ = 0.0092 (or use design charts/approximation)

STEP 7: Check Limits

ρmin = 0.25√f'c/fy = 0.0033 ✓ (ρ > ρmin OK)

ρmax = 0.85β₁(f'c/fy)(εu/(εu+fy/Es)) ≈ 0.018 ✓ (ρ < ρmax OK)

STEP 8: Steel Area (As)

As = ρbd = 0.0092 × 300 × 500 = 1380 mm²

STEP 9: Select Bars

Use 4#25 bars (4×510 = 2040 mm²) or 3#29 (3×645 = 1935 mm²)

Let's use 4T25 (area = 1963 mm² > 1380 required) ✓

STEP 10: Shear Design (Briefly)

Vu at support = WuL/2 = 54.95×6/2 = 164.85 kN

φVc = 0.17λ√f'c bd = 0.17×1×√28×300×500 = 135 kN

Since Vu > φVc, STIRRUPS REQUIRED!

🔢 DESIGN FORMULAS SUMMARY:

Moment Coefficient Method (for rectangular):

Rn = Mu / (φbd²)

ω = ρfy/f'c (reinforcement index)

Rn = ρfy(1 - 0.5ω)

For quick design, use approximation:

As ≈ Mu / (0.9×fy×(d - a/2)) where a = Asfy/(0.85f'cb)

(Iterate 2-3 times for accurate answer)

📌 DESIGN THUMB RULES:

  • ✦ Beam depth d ≈ L/10 to L/12 (simply supported)
  • ✦ Beam width b ≈ d/2 to d/3 (typically 300-400mm)
  • ✦ Typical ρ = 0.01 to 0.035 (1% to 3.5% steel ratio)
  • ✦ Economical range: ρ = 0.015 to 0.025 (1.5% to 2.5%)
  • ✦ Clear cover = 40mm (beams), 25mm (slabs), 75mm (foundations)
  • ✦ Stirrups: T10@200 c/c minimum, closer near supports
  • ✦ Never exceed ρmax - beam will fail BRITTLE (compression failure)

💡 KEY CONCEPTS (Interview Must-Know):

  • ● Under-reinforced (ρ < ρb): Steel yields first → DUCTILE → DESIRABLE
  • ● Over-reinforced (ρ > ρb): Concrete crushes first → BRITTLE → AVOID!
  • ● Balanced (ρ = ρb): Both happen simultaneously → THEORETICAL boundary
  • ● ACI requires tension-controlled sections (εt ≥ 0.005) for φ = 0.9
  • ● Effective depth d = total depth - clear cover - stirrup dia - half bar dia
  • ● Always check ρmin and ρmax after calculating required As
  • ● Whitney stress block: 0.85f'c over depth 'a = β1c'
  • ● β1 = 0.85 for f'c ≤ 28 MPa, reduces by 0.05 for each 7 MPa above
☐ Lesson 7: Shear Design - Stirrup Calculations Pending

📝 What You Will Learn:

  • Concrete shear capacity (Vc) calculation
  • When stirrups are needed
  • Stirrup design procedure (Av, spacing s)
  • Minimum shear reinforcement requirements

💡 Why Do We Need Stirrups?

Imagine diagonal cracks forming at 45° near support:

  • Concrete alone resists some shear through "interlock" and "doweling"
  • But once diagonal crack forms, concrete CANNOT transfer shear across crack
  • Stirrups act like VERTICAL stitches holding two sides together
  • They bridge across the crack and carry the shear force
  • Without stirrups = sudden brittle shear failure (very dangerous!)

🔢 SHEAR DESIGN FORMULAS:

CONCRETE SHEAR CAPACITY (ACI Simplified):

Vc = 0.17 × λ × √f'c × b × d (in Newtons)

Where λ = 1.0 (normal weight concrete)


REQUIRED SHEAR REINFORCEMENT:

If Vu ≤ 0.5φVc → NO stirrups needed theoretically (but provide minimum!)

If Vu > φVc → STIRRUPS REQUIRED


STIRRUP SPACING FORMULA:

s = (Av × fy × d) / Vs

Where Vs = (Vu/φ) - Vc (shear to be resisted by stirrups)

Av = area of both legs of stirrup (for 2-leg: Av = 2 × area of one leg)


MAXIMUM SPACING LIMITS:

s_max = d/2 (when Vs ≤ 4√f'cbd)

s_max = d/4 (when Vs > 4√f'cbd)

s_max ≤ 600mm (absolute maximum)


MINIMUM AV (ACI requirement):

Av,min = 0.062√f'c × (b×s)/fy but not less than 0.35(b×s)/fy

📌 THUMB RULES FOR STIRRUPS:

  • ✦ Use T10 (10mm diameter) or T12 stirrups commonly
  • ✦ 2-leg stirrups standard for beams up to 400mm wide
  • ✦ Start stirrups at d/2 from face of support
  • ✦ Spacing at support: typically 100-150mm c/c
  • ✦ Spacing at mid-span: can increase to 200-300mm c/c (lower shear there)
  • ✦ Never exceed d/2 spacing in regions requiring stirrups
  • ✦ Provide minimum stirrups throughout even if not calculated (code requirement!)
  • ✦ Hook stirrups at 135° around longitudinal bars (not 90°)

💡 KEY CONCEPTS:

  • ● Critical section for shear is at distance 'd' from face of support
  • ● This is because support reaction creates vertical compression that helps resist shear
  • ● Inclined stirrups (bent-up bars) more efficient but harder to construct
  • ● Shear-friction important at interfaces (construction joints)
  • ● Punching shear is TWO-WAY shear (slabs at columns) - different formula!
  • ● Deep beams (L/d < 4) have special shear behavior (strut-and-tie model)
  • ● Torsion combined with shear requires additional reinforcement
☐ Lesson 8: One-Way Slab Design Pending

📝 What You Will Learn:

  • One-way vs Two-way slab identification
  • Minimum thickness requirements
  • Main reinforcement and temperature/shrinkage steel
  • Slab detailing rules

💡 One-Way vs Two-Way - How to Identify:

ONE-WAY SLAB (Ly/Lx ≥ 2):

  • Long span carries load mainly in SHORT direction
  • Behaves like series of beams side-by-side
  • Example: Slab between two parallel beams, corridor slab

TWO-WAY SLAB (Ly/Lx < 2):

  • Load distributed in BOTH directions
  • More efficient, thinner slabs possible
  • Example: Square room slab, flat plate

🔢 ONE-WAY SLAB DESIGN STEPS:

MINIMUM THICKNESS (ACI Table 7.3.1.1):

Simply supported: h_min = L/20

One end continuous: h_min = L/24

Both ends continuous: h_min = L/28

Cantilever: h_min = L/10


DESIGN STRIP WIDTH:

b = 1000mm (design per meter width of slab)


MOMENT CALCULATION (Coefficient Method):

M(+ve) = α × Wu × Ln² (positive moment coefficients)

M(-ve) = α × Wu × Ln² (negative moment at support)

Typical α values: End span +M = 1/12, Support -M = 1/10, Mid +M = 1/16


REINFORCEMENT:

Main steel: As calculated from moment (short direction)

Temp/Shrinkage steel: As = 0.0018bh (Grade 60) or 0.002bh (Grade 40)

Place temp steel perpendicular to main steel (long direction)

📌 SLAB DETAILING RULES:

  • ✦ Main bar spacing ≤ 3h or 450mm (whichever is smaller)
  • ✦ Temp/shrinkage bar spacing ≤ 5h or 450mm
  • ✦ Clear cover for slabs = 20-25mm (exposure dependent)
  • ✦ Use T10 or T12 bars typically for slabs
  • ✦ Crank bars at L/4 for simple supports, L/3 for continuous
  • ✦ Extension of negative reinforcement past support = Ln/3
  • ✦ Minimum slab thickness for fire rating: 2hr = 125mm approx

💡 KEY CONCEPTS:

  • ● One-way slab main reinforcement in SHORT direction only
  • ● Temperature/shrinkage steel prevents cracking due to thermal movement and drying shrinkage
  • ● Slab self-weight is significant - always include in calculations!
  • ● Deflection control often governs slab thickness (not strength)
  • ● For PT slabs later: much thinner spans possible (L/35 to L/45)
  • ● Drop panels/column capitals increase punching shear resistance

⚡ Phase 2: Post-Tensioning Specialist (Months 7-12) 0%
📕 Module 2.1: Fundamentals of Prestressed Concrete ▼
☐ Lesson 10: Introduction to Prestressing - Pre-Tensioning vs Post-Tensioning Pending

📝 Understanding Prestressing:

Prestressing means applying PRE-load to concrete to counteract future service loads. Think of it as "pre-compressing" concrete so that when loads come, net tension is reduced or eliminated.

💡 Simple Analogy - Stack of Books:

Without prestress: Pick up books from ends only → they sag (tension at bottom)

With prestress: Press down on top of books while lifting → they stay straight!

The downward pressure you applied = PRESTRESS FORCE

In concrete: Steel tendons apply this "pressure" internally

🔬 Types of Prestressing:

FeaturePre-TensioningPost-Tensioning
When stressedBefore concrete pouredAfter concrete hardens
LocationPrecast yard onlySite or precast
BondingDirect bond (no ducts)Grouted ducts or unbonded
Length limitsTransport limited (~30m)Any length possible
Common useBridge beams, hollow coreBuildings, parking, slabs

🔢 BASIC PRESTRESS RELATIONSHIP:

Prestress Force: P = Aps × fpe

Aps = Area of prestressing strands (mm²)

fpe = Effective prestress after losses (MPa), typically 0.6-0.7 fpu


TYPICAL STRAND PROPERTIES (0.5" diameter):

Area = 98.7 mm² (single strand)

Ultimate strength fpu = 1860 MPa (Grade 270)

Jacking force = 0.80 fpu = 1488 MPa

Effective after losses ≈ 0.60 fpu = 1116 MPa

📌 THUMB RULES:

  • ✦ Post-tensioning allows LONGER spans than RC (up to 12-15m for slabs)
  • ✦ PT slabs are 20-30% THINNER than equivalent RC slabs
  • ✦ Common strand: 0.5" (12.7mm) diameter, 7-wire low-relaxation
  • ✦ Typical jacking force per strand = 140-160 kN
  • ✦ Effective force after losses = 110-130 kN per strand
  • ✦ Unbonded PT (common in buildings): Greased strands in plastic sheathing
  • ✦ Bonded PT (bridges/heavy): Strands in metal ducts, grouted after stressing

💡 KEY CONCEPTS:

  • ● Prestress introduces INTERNAL compression to counteract tension from loads
  • ● Eccentricity (e) determines WHERE compression acts - controls moment effect
  • ● Pre-tensioning: Strand tensioned between abutments, concrete cast, then released
  • ● Post-tensioning: Concrete cast first with ducts, then strands stressed against hardened concrete
  • ● Transfer length: Length over which prestress transfers to concrete (pre-tension)
  • ● Anchorage zone: High concentration of stresses at anchorage - needs special reinforcement
  • ● Dubai buildings mostly use UNBONDED post-tensioning systems
☐ Lesson 11: LOSSES OF PRESTRESS (CRITICAL TOPIC!) Pending

⚠️ THIS IS THE MOST ASKED INTERVIEW TOPIC IN PT!

Memorize every loss type, cause, and approximate percentage. Interviewers WILL test you on this.

💡 Why Losses Matter:

Scenario: You jack a strand to 160 kN. But after some time, only 120 kN remains effective.

The lost 40 kN = PRESTRESS LOSSES

If you don't account for losses properly:

  • Slab may crack (insufficient compression)
  • Deflections higher than predicted
  • Structure may not meet serviceability requirements

🔢 COMPLETE LOSS FORMULAS:

INSTANTANEOUS LOSSES (happen during stressing):

1. ANCHORAGE SEATING (Wedge Draw-in):

ΔP_anchor = (Ap × Ep × ΔL) / L

ΔL = Wedge draw-in (typically 6mm for wedges)

Ep = 195,000 MPa (modulus of strand)

L = Tendon length

% Loss: Higher for SHORT tendons (up to 10%), lower for long tendons (1-2%)


2. ELASTIC SHORTENING:

Δf_ES = (Es/Ec) × fci × (Ap/Ac) ≈ n × fci × ρp

n = Modular ratio (Es/Ec ≈ 6-7)

fci = Concrete stress at transfer at centroid of CGS

% Loss: Typically 4-7% of initial stress


3. FRICTION LOSS:

Px = P0 × e^(-(Kx + μα))

K = Wobble coefficient (per meter) = 0.001-0.003/m

μ = Curvature friction coeff = 0.05-0.30

x = Distance from jacking end (meters)

α = Cumulative angular change (radians)

% Loss: 1-3% per curve, cumulative along length


TIME-DEPENDENT LOSSES (occur over months/years):

4. CREEP LOSS:

Δf_cr = Ψ(t,t0) × n × fci × ρp × Kcr

Ψ = Creep coefficient (1.5-2.5 typical for UAE conditions)

% Loss: Typically 5-8%


5. SHRINKAGE LOSS:

Δf_sh = εsh × Es

εsh = Shrinkage strain (400-800 × 10⁻⁶ for UAE climate)

Es = 195,000 MPa

% Loss: Typically 4-6%


6. RELAXATION LOSS:

Δf_rel = fpi × (log(t)/10) × R100 (for low-relaxation strand)

R100 = Relaxation class factor (≈2.5% for low-relaxation at 1000 hrs)

% Loss: Typically 2-4% (low-relaxation strand)

📌 TOTAL LOSS SUMMARY (MEMORIZE!):

  • ✦ TOTAL LOSSES for PT slabs ≈ 20-30% of jacking force
  • ✦ Instantaneous (during stressing): ~8-12%
  • ✦ Time-dependent (after stressing): ~12-18%
  • ✦ For preliminary design: Assume 25% loss initially
  • ✦ Shorter tendons: Higher % loss (anchorage dominant)
  • ✦ Longer tendons: Friction loss more significant
  • ✦ UAE hot climate: Creep and shrinkage may be HIGHER than temperate regions
  • ✦ Always calculate exact losses for final design!

💡 INTERVIEW QUESTIONS ON LOSSES:

  • ● Q: Which loss is highest? A: Usually creep or friction (depends on situation)
  • ● Q: Which loss happens FIRST? A: Elastic shortening (during transfer)
  • ● Q: Can we reduce friction loss? A: Yes - stress from BOTH ends for long tendons
  • ● Q: What is wobble? A: Unintentional misalignment of duct/tendon
  • ● Q: Why use low-relaxation strand? A: Reduces relaxation loss significantly
  • ● Q: Does grouting affect losses? A: Grout doesn't affect losses (already occurred)
  • ● Q: How to measure actual losses? A: Elongation measurement during jacking
☐ Lesson 12: LOAD BALANCING METHOD (Core PT Concept) Pending

📝 THE MOST IMPORTANT DESIGN CONCEPT IN PT!

Load balancing explains WHY prestressing works so well. Understand this deeply.

💡 The Magic of Load Balancing:

Think of a parabolic tendon like an inverted arch:

  • A tightly stretched string wants to straighten
  • If forced into curved (parabolic) shape, it pushes UPWARD on whatever holds it in curve
  • This UPWARD push = "Balanced Load" that counteracts gravity
  • If balanced load = dead load → slab sees almost NO load under DL only!
  • Only remaining unbalanced load = Live Load portion

Result: Much smaller deflections, minimal cracking, thinner sections possible!

🔢 LOAD BALANCING FORMULAS:

wb = 8Pe/L²

Where:

wb = Balanced load (upward force per meter) in kN/m or kPa

P = Effective prestress force (kN)

e = Drape (eccentricity) in meters (high point to low point)

L = Span length in meters


HARPED TENDON (triangular profile):

wb = 4Pe/L² (different factor for harped/kinked profile)


DESIGN PHILOSOPHY:

Option A: Balance 100% DL + some LL (conservative, less deflection)

Option B: Balance 70-80% DL only (economical, common practice)

Unbalanced moment = (Total load - Balanced load) effects

📌 LOAD BALANCING THUMB RULES:

  • ✦ More drape (e) = More balancing effect = More efficient
  • ✦ Shorter span (L) = More balancing effect for same Pe
  • ✦ Typical drape e ≈ 0.9 × slab thickness (to maintain cover)
  • ✦ Balance 65-80% of DL for typical flat plates (good starting point)
  • ✦ Higher PT force needed for longer spans or heavier loads
  • ✦ Don't over-balance (>100% DL) - causes camber problems!
  • ✦ At transfer: Only balance self-weight (superimposed DL not present yet)

💡 KEY CONCEPTS:

  • ● Primary Moment: P × e (direct effect of eccentricity)
  • ● Secondary Moment: Indeterminate effects in continuous members
  • ● Total Moment = Primary + Secondary (both must be considered!)
  • ● Equivalent load method: Replace tendon with upward distributed load
  • ● Concordant tendon: Produces NO secondary moments (theoretical ideal)
  • ● Linear transformation: Can shift tendon profile without changing C-line
  • ● Load balancing makes complex PT analysis SIMPLE and intuitive
🏗️ Module 2.2: PT Slab Design Methodology ▼
☐ Lesson 13: PT One-Way System Design Pending

📝 One-Way PT Systems:

Band-beam and slab system, or one-way PT slabs spanning between beams/walls.

🔢 DESIGN PROCEDURE:

STEP 1: Select Thickness

One-way PT slab: h = L/35 to L/40

PT Band beam: h = L/25 to L/30


STEP 2: Define Tendon Profile

Parabolic profile: High at supports, low at midspan

e (drape) = available depth minus covers


STEP 3: Calculate Balanced Load

wb = 8Pe/L² per tendon

Total wb = sum of all tendons


STEP 4: Analyze for Unbalanced Moments

Unbalanced load = (DL + LL) - wb

Analyze as regular RC member for unbalanced load


STEP 5: Stress Checks (ACI 18.4)

At Transfer: ft ≤ 0.25√f'ci (tension), fc ≤ 0.6f'ci (compression)

At Service: Decompression allowed, fc ≤ 0.45f'c


STEP 6: Flexural Design

Add bonded reinforcement if needed (φMn ≥ Mu)

Min bonded rebar: As = 0.001Ag (one-way slabs)

📌 THUMB RULES:

  • ✦ One-way PT slab span up to 10-12m practical
  • ✦ Tendon spacing typically 1000-1500mm c/c
  • ✦ Use 3-5 strands per tendon typical for slabs
  • ✦ Band beams: 6-12 strands common
  • ✦ Always check deflection (long-term especially)
☐ Lesson 14: PT Two-Way Flat Plate Design (Most Used in UAE!) Pending

🏢 THIS IS WHAT YOU'LL SEE MOST IN DUBAI BUILDINGS!

Flat plates are the most common PT system in UAE residential towers. Master this thoroughly.

💡 Why Flat Plates Dominate in Dubai:

  • Flat soffit (no beams) → Easy MEP coordination
  • Reduced floor-to-floor height → More rentable floors
  • Faster construction (simple formwork)
  • Clean ceiling appearance (or easy false ceiling)
  • Flexible interior layout (no beam protrusions)

🔢 FLAT PLATE DESIGN STEPS:

THICKNESS SELECTION:

h = L/40 to L/45 (without drop panels)

h = L/45 to L/50 (with drop panels)

Minimum h = 150mm (practical), typically 180-250mm


TENDON LAYOUT OPTIONS:

Option A: Banded-Distributed (MOST COMMON)

- One direction: Tendons BANDED over columns (like hidden beams)

- Other direction: Tendons DISTRIBUTED uniformly

- Advantage: Easy to avoid MEP conflicts


Option B: Uniform Both Directions

- Equal spacing in both directions

- More optimal theoretically

- Harder to coordinate penetrations


STRIP DEFINITION (ACI 8.13):

Column Strip: Width = 0.25L1 to 0.25L2 each side of column (min L2/4)

Middle Strip: Between column strips


PUNCHING SHEAR CHECK (CRITICAL!):

φVc ≥ Vu at d/2 from column face

Vc = Min of: 0.33√f'c, (2+4/βc)√f'c, (αsd/d+2)√f'c × bo×d

If inadequate: Add shear studs, increase slab thickness, add drop panel/capital

📌 FLAT PLATE THUMB RULES:

  • ✦ Typical bay size: 6m × 6m to 9m × 9m (optimal 7-8m)
  • ✦ Slab thickness: 200-220mm most common for residential
  • ✦ Tendons: 3-5 strands per tendon, spaced 1000-1200mm
  • ✦ Always check punching shear FIRST (often governs design!)
  • ✦ Corner columns critical (two-way unbalanced moment)
  • ✦ Edge columns need special attention (less surrounding slab help)
  • ✦ Opening near columns reduces perimeter - very dangerous!

💡 KEY CONCEPTS:

  • ● Column strip takes 60-75% of total moment
  • ● Middle strip takes 25-40% of total moment
  • ● Negative moment over columns → Top reinforcement needed
  • ● Positive moment at mid-span → Bottom reinforcement needed
  • ● PT contribution to punching shear: Vertical component of tendon force
  • ● Shear reinforcement options: Stud rails (popular), stirrups, shear heads
  • ● Deflection control often governs thickness selection

📊 Module 3.1: Column Design ▼
☐ Lesson 16: Short Column Design (Axial + Moment) Pending

📝 Column Design Fundamentals:

Columns carry vertical loads from beams/slabs to foundation. Unlike beams, columns have AXIAL force PLUS bending moment simultaneously.

🔢 COLUMN DESIGN FORMULAS:

NOMINAL AXIAL CAPACITY:

Pn = 0.85fc(Ag - Ast) + fyAst

φPn = 0.65 × Pn (for tied columns)

φPn = 0.70 × Pn (for spiral columns)


INTERACTION DIAGRAM (P-M):

Columns designed using interaction diagrams showing combined P-M capacity

Points on curve represent safe combinations of axial load and moment

Inside curve = SAFE, Outside curve = FAIL


MINIMUM REINFORCEMENT:

ρmin = 0.01 (1% of gross area) - Ag

ρmax = 0.08 (8% of gross area) - Ag

Practical range: 1% to 4%


TIE REQUIREMENTS:

Tie spacing: 16db (longitudinal bar dia) or 48dtie or min column dimension

Every corner bar must have lateral support

Spacing ≤ 150mm where splices occur

📌 COLUMN THUMB RULES:

  • ✦ Typical column sizes: 300×300 to 800×800mm
  • ✦ Higher floors = Smaller columns (less load)
  • ✦ Use minimum 4 bars for small columns, 6+ for larger
  • ✦ T12-T25 typical bar sizes for columns
  • ✦ T10 ties common, T12 for heavy columns
  • ✦ Keep reinforcement SYMMETRICAL (equal both faces)
  • ✦ Lap splices at mid-height (minimum moment region)

💡 KEY CONCEPTS:

  • ● Short column: Failure by material crushing (no buckling effect)
  • ● Slender column: Buckling amplifies moment (need magnification)
  • ● Slenderness ratio: kl/r < 22 = short (braced frame)
  • ● Eccentricity: Axial load applied offset from centroid = moment
  • ● Minimum eccentricity: L/500 or 20mm (code minimum)
  • ● Spiral columns stronger than tied (confined concrete)
  • ● Biaxial bending: Moment about both axes (corner columns)
🧱 Module 3.2: Shear Wall Design Basics ▼
☐ Lesson 17: Introduction to Shear Walls Pending

📝 Shear Wall Fundamentals:

Shear walls are vertical RC elements that resist lateral loads (wind, seismic). Essential for tall buildings in Dubai (wind-dominated design).

💡 Why Shear Walls in Dubai Towers:

  • High-rise buildings act like vertical cantilevers under wind
  • Columns alone cannot resist huge wind moments
  • Shear walls provide STIFFNESS (control lateral drift)
  • Also provide STRENGTH (resist shear forces)
  • Usually located around elevator cores/stairwells
  • Create "tube" effect for efficient lateral resistance

🔢 SHEAR WALL BASICS:

TYPES BY SHAPE:

Rectangular (simple wall)

Flanged (L-shaped, T-shaped, U-shaped, C-shaped)

Coupled (two walls connected by beams)


BEHAVIOR:

Flexural behavior: Wall acts like vertical cantilever

Shear behavior: In-plane shear resistance

Slender walls: Bending dominated (flexural design governs)

Stocky walls: Shear dominated (shear design governs)


BOUNDARY ELEMENTS:

Edges of walls with concentrated reinforcement

Confined zones similar to column detailing

Required when compression stress exceeds threshold

📌 SHEAR WALL THUMB RULES:

  • ✦ Wall thickness: 200-400mm typical (150mm minimum)
  • ✦ Thickness may increase down building (higher forces)
  • ✦ Horizontal reinforcement: 0.0025Ag minimum
  • ✦ Vertical reinforcement: 0.0025Ag minimum
  • ✦ Two layers of reinforcement (each face) for thick walls
  • ✦ Boundary elements detailed like columns (ties, confinement)
  • ✦ Coupling beams heavily reinforced (often diagonally)
🏠 Module 3.3: Foundation Design ▼
☐ Lesson 18: Isolated Footing Design Pending

📝 Footing Design - Transferring Building Loads to Ground:

Foundations distribute column/wall loads to soil at safe bearing pressure. Most basic and common foundation type.

🔢 FOOTING DESIGN STEPS:

STEP 1: Determine Size from Bearing Capacity

Area required = Pu / (qna × FS) or Service Load / SBC

Select square or rectangular dimensions


STEP 2: Net Upward Pressure

qu = (Pu/Area) - (self-weight of footing/area)

Or approximately: qu = Pu/Area (for preliminary)


STEP 3: Depth from ONE-WAY SHEAR (Wide Beam)

Critical section at d from face of column

Vu = qu × L × (B/2 - c/2 - d)

Require: φVc ≥ Vu where Vc = 0.17√f'c × L × d


STEP 4: Depth from PUNCHING SHEAR (Two-Way)

Critical section at d/2 from column face (perimeter bo)

Vu = Pu - qu × (c+d)² (for square column)

φVc = φ × Min(...) × bo × d


STEP 5: Flexural Reinforcement

Mu = qu × L × (B-c)²/8 (for cantilever strip)

Design as wide beam: As = Mu / (0.9×fy×(d-a/2))


STEP 6: Development Length Check

Ld available ≥ Ld required

If not: Use hooks or increase depth

📌 FOUNDATION THUMB RULES:

  • ✦ Typical footing depth: 400-600mm for light structures
  • ✦ Clear cover: 75mm (aggressive ground exposure)
  • ✦ SBC in Dubai coastal: 150-250 kPa (sand)
  • ✦ SBC Sharjah inland: May be 250-500 kPa (better soil)
  • ✦ Minimum footing size: 600×600mm (constructability)
  • ✦ T16 minimum bar size for footings
  • ✦ Always check settlement (serviceability) not just bearing
