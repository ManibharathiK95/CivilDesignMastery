Module 1.1: Strength of Materials ▼
Lesson 1: Stress & Strain
Understanding how materials behave under load - the basics of engineering mechanics

📖 What is Stress?

Stress is the internal force per unit area inside a material when external load is applied.

Formula: σ = P / A
Where: σ = Stress (MPa or N/mm²), P = Force (N), A = Area (mm²)
Example: A column carries 500 kN load. Column size is 300mm × 300mm.
Area A = 300 × 300 = 90,000 mm²
Stress σ = 500,000 N / 90,000 mm² = 5.56 MPa

📖 What is Strain?

Strain is the deformation (change in length) divided by original length. It has no units - it's a ratio.

Formula: ε = δ / L
Where: ε = Strain (no units), δ = Change in length (mm), L = Original length (mm)
Example: A steel rod of length 2000mm stretches by 2mm under load.
Strain ε = 2 / 2000 = 0.001 (or 0.1%)

📖 Hooke's Law

Within elastic limit, stress is directly proportional to strain. The constant is called Young's Modulus (E).

Formula: E = σ / ε
For Steel: E ≈ 200,000 MPa | For Concrete: E ≈ 25,000-35,000 MPa
📌 Thumb Rules:
• Concrete can take much more stress in compression than tension
• Steel takes equal stress in tension and compression
• Strain in concrete at failure: ~0.003 (compression), ~0.00015 (tension cracking)
• Always check units! 1 MPa = 1 N/mm²
💡 Key Concepts:
• Elastic Limit: Material returns to original shape after removing load
• Yield Point: Permanent deformation starts
• Ultimate Strength: Maximum stress material can take before breaking
• Factor of Safety: Design strength / Actual strength (usually 1.5 to 3.0)
🔢 Formulas to Remember Always:
1. σ = P / A (Stress)
2. ε = δ / L (Strain)
3. E = σ / ε (Young's Modulus)
4. δ = PL / AE (Elongation formula)
Lesson 2: Shear Force & Bending Moment Diagrams
Drawing SFD and BMD - essential skill every engineer must know

📖 What is Shear Force (V)?

Shear force is the internal force that tries to slide one part of beam relative to another part. Think of scissors cutting paper - that's shear!

Sign Convention: Upward force on LEFT side = Positive (+) shear
Relationship: dV/dx = w (load intensity)

📖 What is Bending Moment (M)?

Bending moment is the internal moment that tries to bend the beam. It causes tension on one face and compression on other.

Sign Convention: Sagging (smile shape) = Positive (+) moment
Relationship: dM/dx = V (shear force)
Example: Simply Supported Beam with UDL
Beam span L = 6m, UDL w = 10 kN/m

Reactions: RA = RB = wL/2 = 10×6/2 = 30 kN each

Max Shear (at supports): V = ±30 kN
Max Moment (at center): M = wL²/8 = 10×36/8 = 45 kNm

SFD shape: Linear from +30kN to -30kN
BMD shape: Parabolic curve, max 45kNm at center
📌 Thumb Rules for SFD/BMD:
• Point load → SFD jumps vertically, BMD has sharp corner
• UDL → SFD slopes linearly, BMD curves parabolically
• Moment is ZERO at simple supports and FREE ends
• Where SFD = 0, BMD has MAX or MIN value
• For cantilever: Max moment at FIXED support
💡 Key Concepts:
• SFD tells you where shear is critical (design stirrups here)
• BMD tells you where bending is critical (design main rebar here)
• Area under SFD = change in BMD value
• Always draw SFD first, then integrate to get BMD
🔢 Must-Remember Formulas:
• Simply supported + UDL: Mmax = wL²/8, Vmax = wL/2
• Simply supported + Point load at center: Mmax = PL/4, Vmax = P/2
• Cantilever + UDL: Mmax = wL²/2 (at fixed end), Vmax = wL
• Cantilever + Point load at free end: Mmax = PL (at fixed end), Vmax = P
Lesson 3: Bending Stresses in Beams
How to calculate stress distribution across beam depth

📖 Flexure Formula (Bending Equation)

This is THE most important formula in beam design. It relates bending moment to stress in the beam.

The Formula: σ = My / I
Or: σ = M / Z (where Z = I/y, Section Modulus)

Where:
σ = Bending stress (MPa)
M = Bending moment (Nmm or kNm - be careful with units!)
y = Distance from neutral axis to point where you calculate stress (mm)
I = Moment of Inertia about neutral axis (mm⁴)
Example: Rectangular Beam
Beam: b=300mm wide, h=600mm deep
Moment M = 150 kNm = 150×10⁶ Nmm
f'c = 28 MPa concrete

Step 1: Find I (Moment of Inertia)
I = bh³/12 = 300 × 600³ / 12 = 5.4 × 10⁹ mm⁴

Step 2: Find y_max (distance to extreme fiber)
y_max = h/2 = 300mm

Step 3: Calculate max stress
σ_max = My/I = (150×10⁶)(300)/(5.4×10⁹) = 8.33 MPa

Check: 8.33 MPa < 28 MPa ✓ OK!
📌 Thumb Rules:
• Neutral axis passes through centroid of cross-section
• For rectangular section: NA is at mid-depth (h/2)
• Max stress occurs at TOP and BOTTOM fibers (y is maximum there)
• Stress is ZERO at neutral axis
• Tension at bottom, Compression at top for positive (sagging) moment
• Z (Section Modulus) = I/y_max - use this for quick calculations
💡 Key Concepts:
• Moment of Inertia (I) measures resistance to bending
• Larger I = stronger beam against bending
• Increasing depth is MORE effective than width for increasing I
• I ∝ h³ (so doubling depth increases I by 8 times!)
🔢 Common I Values (Memorize these):
• Rectangle (b×h): I = bh³/12, Z = bh²/6
• Circle (diameter d): I = πd⁴/64, Z = πd³/32
• Triangle (base b, height h): I = bh³/36
Lesson 4: Shear Stresses in Beams
Understanding shear stress distribution - critical for stirrup design

📖 Shear Stress Formula

Shear stress varies across the beam depth - it's NOT uniform like bending stress at extreme fibers!

Formula: τ = VQ / (Ib)

Where:
τ = Shear stress at any point (MPa)
V = Shear force at that section (N)
Q = First moment of area above/below the point (mm³)
I = Moment of inertia of full section (mm⁴)
b = Width of beam at that point (mm)
Example: Rectangular Beam Shear Stress
Beam: b=300mm, h=600mm
Shear force V = 100 kN = 100,000 N
I = 5.4×10⁹ mm⁴ (from previous lesson)

Max shear stress (at NA, y=0):
For rectangular section: τ_max = 1.5 × V/A (simple formula!)
A = 300×600 = 180,000 mm²
τ_max = 1.5 × 100,000 / 180,000 = 0.83 MPa

Note: τ_max occurs at NEUTRAL AXIS, not at top/bottom!
τ = 0 at top and bottom fibers
📌 Thumb Rules:
• Max shear stress is at Neutral Axis (center of depth)
• Shear stress = 0 at top and bottom extreme fibers
• For rectangular beam: τ_max = 1.5 × (V/A) - average shear × 1.5
• For I-beam/T-beam: most shear carried by WEB, very little in flanges
• Shear stress distribution is PARABOLIC for rectangular sections
💡 Key Concepts:
• Q represents "area moment" - area × distance from NA to centroid of that area
• At NA: Q is maximum → so τ is maximum there
• This is why we design stirrups based on shear at NA region
• Diagonal tension from shear causes 45° cracks if not properly reinforced
🔢 Quick Formulas:
• Rectangular: τ_max = 3V/(2A) = 1.5V/A
• Circular: τ_max = 4V/(3A) = 1.333V/A
• I-beam (approx): τ_max ≈ V/(web_depth × web_thickness)
Module 1.2: Reinforced Concrete Basics ▼
Lesson 5: Introduction to RC Design & ACI 318
Limit state method, safety factors, and why we use ACI code in UAE

📖 Why Reinforced Concrete?

  • Concrete is strong in COMPRESSION but weak in TENSION (only ~10% of compressive strength)
  • Steel is strong in BOTH tension AND compression
  • RC = Concrete + Steel → Best of both worlds!
  • Steel bars are placed where TENSION occurs (bottom of beam for positive moment)

📖 Limit State Method (LSM)

We don't design for actual loads - we design for FACTORED loads (increased for safety).

Load Combinations (ACI 318):
U = 1.4D (Dead Load only)
U = 1.2D + 1.6L (Dead + Live - MOST COMMON!)
U = 1.2D + 1.0W + 0.5L (with Wind)

D = Dead Load (self-weight, permanent fixtures)
L = Live Load (people, furniture, movable items)
Example: Factored Load Calculation
Slab dead load = 5 kPa, Live load = 3 kPa

Factored load Wu = 1.2D + 1.6L
Wu = 1.2(5) + 1.6(3) = 6 + 4.8 = 10.8 kPa

We design for 10.8 kPa, not actual 8 kPa. This gives us safety margin!
📌 Thumb Rules:
• φ (phi factor) = strength reduction factor
• φ = 0.9 for flexure (tension-controlled)
• φ = 0.75 for shear and torsion
• φ = 0.65 for compression-controlled sections
• Always use φMn ≥ Mu (design moment capacity ≥ factored moment demand)
💡 Why ACI 318 in UAE/Dubai?
• Most PT consultancies in Dubai follow ACI code
• British Code (Eurocode) used by some older firms
• ACI is more common for PT buildings specifically
• As a PT specialist, focus on ACI 318 primarily
🔢 Core Equations:
• Demand: Mu = factored moment (from analysis)
• Capacity: φMn = φ × (design strength)
• Safety check: φMn ≥ Mu (capacity must exceed demand)
Lesson 6: Singly Reinforced Beam Design
Step-by-step method to design a rectangular beam with tension steel only

📖 Design Steps (Memorize this flow!)

GIVEN: Mu (factored moment), b (width), f'c (concrete strength), fy (steel strength), cover

Design Procedure:
Step 1: Assume effective depth d (usually d = h - cover - stirrup - half bar diameter)
Step 2: Calculate Rn = Mu / (φbd²)
Step 3: Find ρ (reinforcement ratio) from Rn equation OR use approximation
Step 4: Check ρ_min ≤ ρ ≤ ρ_max
Step 5: Calculate As = ρ × b × d
Step 6: Select bar number and count
Step 7: Design shear reinforcement (stirrups)
Complete Example:
Given: Mu = 200 kNm, b = 300mm, f'c = 28 MPa, fy = 420 MPa, cover = 40mm

Step 1: Assume h = 500mm, then d = 500 - 40 - 10 - 12 = 438mm ≈ 440mm

Step 2: Rn = (200×10⁶) / (0.9 × 300 × 440²) = 3.82 MPa

Step 3: Using simplified formula: ρ = (0.85f'c/fy)[1 - √(1 - 2Rn/(0.85f'c))]
ρ = (0.85×28/420)[1 - √(1 - 2×3.82/(0.85×28))]
ρ = 0.0567[1 - √(1-0.321)] = 0.0567 × 0.175 = 0.00993

Step 4: ρ_min = 1.4/fy = 0.0033, ρ_max ≈ 0.021 (for f'c=28, fy=420)
0.0033 ≤ 0.00993 ≤ 0.021 ✓ OK!

Step 5: As = 0.00993 × 300 × 440 = 1311 mm²

Step 6: Use 4T20 bars (As = 1257 mm²) or 3T25 (1473 mm²)
Try 3T25 = 1473 mm² > 1311 mm² ✓ OK!
💡 Important Notes:
• Always round UP the steel area (never go below calculated)
• Check minimum spacing between bars (for concrete placement)
• Single layer of bars preferred (if possible) for better bond
• If As is too large, consider increasing beam depth instead
🔢 Key Formulas:
• Rn = Mu / (φbd²) - Required nominal moment coefficient
• ω = ρfy/(0.85f'c) - reinforcement index (keep ω ≤ 0.85β1 for ductility)
• β1 = 0.85 for f'c ≤ 28 MPa, reduces by 0.05 for each 7 MPa above 28
• As_min = larger of (1.4bd/fy) or (0.25√f'c/fy × bd)
Lesson 7: Shear Design (Stirrups)
How to design stirrups to resist diagonal shear cracks

📖 Why Shear Reinforcement?

Concrete alone can resist some shear (Vc). When Vu exceeds φVc, we need STIRRUPS (shear reinforcement) to carry the excess.

Basic Equation:
φVn ≥ Vu
Where: Vn = Vc + Vs
Vc = Concrete shear capacity
Vs = Stirrup shear capacity

📖 Step-by-Step Shear Design

Procedure:
Step 1: Calculate Vu (factored shear force) at distance d from support
Step 2: Calculate Vc (concrete capacity)
For members without axial load: Vc = 0.17λ√f'c × bw × d (ACI Eq. 22.5.5.1)
λ = 1.0 for normal weight concrete

Step 3: If Vu ≤ 0.5φVc → NO stirrups needed (minimum only)
If Vu > φVc → calculate Vs needed
Vs = (Vu/φ) - Vc

Step 4: Design stirrups: Av = 2 × (area of one leg of stirrup)
Spacing s = (Av × fy × d) / Vs

Step 5: Check spacing limits (d/2 or 600mm, whichever is less)
Example:
Beam: bw=300mm, d=440mm, f'c=28MPa, fy=420MPa
Vu at d from support = 150 kN

Step 2: Vc = 0.17 × 1.0 × √28 × 300 × 440 = 118.8 kN
φVc = 0.75 × 118.8 = 89.1 kN

Step 3: Vu (150) > φVc (89.1) → Need stirrups!
Vs = (150/0.75) - 118.8 = 200 - 118.8 = 81.2 kN

Step 4: Use T10 stirrups (2 legs), Av = 2 × 78.5 = 157 mm²
s = (157 × 420 × 440) / (81.2 × 1000) = 357 mm

Step 5: Max spacing = d/2 = 220mm
Use T10 @ 220mm c/c (or say T10 @ 200 c/c for practical)
💡 Practical Tips:
• Use T10 or T12 stirrups commonly (T8 for slabs/light beams)
• Standard spacing: 100mm, 150mm, 200mm, 225mm, 250mm, 300mm
• Closer spacing near support (high shear), can increase towards mid-span
• Minimum stirrups: even if not needed, provide T10@max spacing for crack control
🔢 Quick Reference:
• Vc ≈ 0.17√f'c × bw × d (simplified)
• s = Avfyd/Vs (spacing formula)
• Av = 2 × (π×dia²/4) for 2-legged stirrup
• Min Av/fv = 0.062√f'c/fy (minimum reinforcement ratio)
Lesson 8: One-Way Slab Design
Designing slabs that span in one direction - common in PT band-beam systems

📖 What is One-Way Slab?

When slab spans mainly in ONE direction (Ly/Lx ≥ 2), it's called one-way slab. Load transfers to two opposite beams/walls.

Thumb Rule for Slab Thickness:
• One-way slab: min h = L/20 (simply supported) or L/24 (continuous) or L/28 (cantilever)
• Example: Span 4.5m simply supported → h ≥ 4500/20 = 225mm (use 225 or 230mm)
Complete Slab Design Example:
Clear span Ln = 4.5m, LL = 3 kPa, Floor finish = 1.5 kPa
f'c = 28 MPa, fy = 420 MPa, Simply supported

Step 1: Thickness
h = L/20 = 4500/20 = 225mm → Use 230mm
d = 230 - 20(clear cover) - 10(bar/2) - 6(stirrup?) = assume d = 205mm

Step 2: Loads
Self-weight = 0.23 × 25 = 5.75 kPa
Total DL = 5.75 + 1.5 = 7.25 kPa
LL = 3 kPa
Wu = 1.2(7.25) + 1.6(3) = 8.7 + 4.8 = 13.5 kPa

Step 3: Moment (per 1m width)
Mu = WuLn²/8 = 13.5 × 4.5² / 8 = 34.2 kNm/m

4: Steel (per 1m width, b=1000mm)
Rn = (34.2×10⁶)/(0.9×1000×205²) = 0.906 MPa
Calculate ρ... → As ≈ 430 mm²/m
Use T12 @ 250mm c/c (As = 452 mm²/m) ✓

5: Temperature/Shrinkage Steel (other direction)
As_min = 0.0018 × b × h = 0.0018 × 1000 × 230 = 414 mm²/m
Use T10 @ 180mm c/c (As = 436 mm²/m) ✓
💡 Key Points:
• Main steel in SHORT direction (spanning direction)
• Temp/shrinkage steel in LONG direction (distribution steel)
• Main steel at BOTTOM for positive (sagging) moment
• Main steel at TOP over supports for negative (hogging) moment
• Check deflection: L/250 for total, L/360 for live load deflection
🔢 Slab Quick Formulas:
• Mu (SS) = WL²/8 | Mu (Fixed ends) = WL/12 (support), WL/24 (mid)
• As_main = from flexural design
• As_temp = 0.0018bh (or 0.0015bh for Grade 60)
• Max spacing of main bars: min(3h, 450mm)
• Max spacing of temp bars: min(5h, 450mm)
Module 1.3: AutoCAD for Structural Drafting ▼
Lesson 9: Intelligent Drafting Practices
Beyond drawing lines - understanding structural drawings as an engineer

📖 Think Like an Engineer While Drafting

As a draftsman transitioning to designer, your CAD skills give you an advantage. But you must draft INTELLIGENTLY, not just copy-paste.

📌 Intelligent Drafting Rules:
• Understand what you're drawing: Don't just copy lines - know WHY that tendon goes there
• Read structural notes: Every note has engineering reason behind it
• Standardize details: Create reusable blocks for anchorage, couplers, typical sections
• Layer discipline: Use proper layers (PT_TENDONS, REBAR_MAIN, REBAR_STIRRUP, DIMENSIONS, TEXT)
• Xrefs wisely: Use XREFs for grid/architectural background, never explode them
💡 PT-Specific Drafting Knowledge:
• Tendon profile shows HIGH points at supports, LOW points at midspan
• Anchorage zone needs special detailing (bursting reinforcement shown clearly)
• Strip layout plan shows column strips vs middle strips
• Clearance zones for MEP penetrations around tendons
• Coupler locations at construction joints must be clear
Practical Exercise:
Take any PT tendon layout from your current project and analyze:
1. Why is tendon HIGH at this location? (Negative moment zone)
2. Why is tendon LOW here? (Positive moment zone)
3. What is the drape (eccentricity difference)?
4. Where are the anchorage pockets located?
5. How does tendon avoid column/beam intersections?

If you can answer all 5 questions, you're thinking like a designer!
🔢 Drawing Standards to Know:
• Text height: 2.5mm (general), 3-5mm (titles)
• Dimension precision: 1mm for metric drawings
• Scale: 1:50 for plans, 1:20 for details, 1:10 for complex details
• Line weights: Thick (0.5-0.7mm) for outlines, thin (0.18-0.25mm) for dimensions

Module 2.1: Fundamentals of Prestressing ▼
Lesson 10: Introduction to Post-Tensioning
What is PT? How does it work? Pre-tensioning vs Post-tensioning

📖 What is Post-Tensioning (PT)?

Prestressing means applying internal forces to concrete BEFORE it carries external loads. This puts concrete into PRE-COMPRESSION, so when loads come, the tension is reduced or eliminated.

💡 Simple Analogy:
Imagine picking up several books. If you press down on them first (pre-compress), then lift - they stay together better!

Same principle: We COMPRESS concrete first with high-strength steel strands. Then when gravity loads try to TENDON the bottom (cause tension), our pre-compression fights against it.
📌 Pre-tensioning vs Post-tensioning:
PRE-TENSIONING:
• Strand tensioned BEFORE concrete is cast
• Strand bonded directly to concrete
• Used in PRECAST factories (hollow core slabs, bridge beams)
• Limited to straight or slightly draped profiles

POST-TENSIONING:
• Strand placed in duct/sheath, cast into concrete FIRST
• THEN tensioned AFTER concrete hardens (usually 3-7 days)
• Used in CAST-IN-SITU construction (buildings, parking, water tanks)
• Can have ANY profile (parabolic, harped, custom)
• THIS IS WHAT WE FOCUS ON FOR BUILDINGS!
Why PT in Buildings?
Conventional RC 200mm slab can span ~6m efficiently
PT 200mm slab can span ~10-12m efficiently!

Benefits:
• Longer spans (fewer columns → more open space)
• Thinner sections (less concrete → lighter building → cheaper foundation)
• Better crack control (slabs stay flatter, less deflection)
• Reduced overall building height (more floors in same height)
• Faster construction (PT allows early formwork stripping)
🔢 Basic PT Terminology:
• Tendon: Bundle of 0.5" strands (typically 3 to 19 strands per tendon)
• Strand: 7-wire high-strength steel wire (Grade 270 = 1860 MPa ultimate)
• Jacking force (Pi): Initial force applied during stressing
• Effective force (Pe): Force remaining after ALL losses (~70-80% of Pi)
• Eccentricity (e): Distance from CG of strand to CG of concrete section
Lesson 11: Losses of Prestress (CRITICAL!)
The 6 types of losses - interviewers WILL ask this!

📖 Why Do We Lose Prestress?

When we apply jacking force Pi, we don't get to keep it all. Some force is lost due to various reasons. Understanding losses is CRUCIAL for PT design.

Total Losses typically = 20-30% of initial jacking force

📖 INSTANTANEOUS LOSSES (Happen immediately during stressing)

Loss 1: Elastic Shortening (ES)
When we tension the tendon, concrete gets compressed (shortens). Since strand is bonded to concrete, it also shortens slightly → loses some tension.

Magnitude: Typically 4-7% of initial stress
Formula: Δfes = n × fci × (Ap/Ac) ≈ (Es/Ec) × fci × ρp
Where n = modular ratio, fci = concrete stress at transfer
Loss 2: Anchorage Seating (Draw-in/Wedge Seating)
When jack is released, wedges pull INTO anchor casting to grip strand. This pulls strand back slightly (about 6mm). Shorter tendons lose MORE % than long tendons.

Magnitude: 1-4% of initial stress (higher for short tendons)
Formula: ΔP = (Ap × Ep × ΔL) / L
Where ΔL = wedge draw-in (typically 6mm), L = tendon length
Anchorage Loss Example:
Tendon length L = 10m = 10,000mm
Draw-in ΔL = 6mm
Ep = 195,000 MPa
Ap = 140 mm² (one strand)

ΔP = (140 × 195000 × 6) / 10000 = 16,380 N = 16.38 kN per strand
If Pi per strand = 160 kN, loss = 16.38/160 = 10.2%!

Now same tendon but L = 30m:
ΔP = (140 × 195000 × 6) / 30000 = 5.46 kN → Only 3.4% loss!
Conclusion: Shorter tendons lose more % from anchorage!
Loss 3: Friction Loss
As strand rubs against duct interior (wobble effect) and curves (curvature effect), friction resists the jacking force. Force decreases along tendon length from jacking end.

Magnitude: 3-10% depending on tendon length and profile curvature
Formula (MEMORIZE THIS!):
Px = P0 × e^(-(Kx + μα))
Where:
Px = Force at distance x from jacking end
P0 = Jacking force
K = Wobble coeff (0.001-0.003 per meter)
μ = Curvature friction coeff (0.05-0.30)
α = Cumulative angular change (radians)
x = Distance from jacking end (meters)
Friction Loss Example:
Parabolic tendon, span = 12m, stressed from one end only
P0 = 1500 kN, K = 0.002/m, μ = 0.20
Total angle α = 2 × arctan(2×drape/span) ≈ 0.10 rad (for 200mm drape)
x = 12m (far end)

Px = 1500 × e^(-(0.002×12 + 0.20×0.10))
Px = 1500 × e^(-0.044) = 1500 × 0.957 = 1435.5 kN
Loss = 1500 - 1435.5 = 64.5 kN = 4.3%

📖 TIME-DEPENDENT LOSSES (Happen gradually over months/years)

Loss 4: Creep
Under sustained compressive stress, concrete slowly deforms (creeps). This additional shortening relaxes the strand tension.

Magnitude: 4-6% of initial stress
Formula: Δfcr = n × fci × Ψ(t,t0) × ρp
Ψ = creep coefficient (1.5-2.5 typical for buildings)
Loss 5: Shrinkage
Concrete shrinks as it dries over time. This shrinkage shortens the member and relaxes the strand.

Magnitude: 5-8% of initial stress
Formula: Δfsh = εsh × Es
εsh = shrinkage strain (400-800 × 10^-6 typical)
Es = 195,000 MPa (strand modulus)
Loss 6: Relaxation
Even if strand length stays constant, high-strength steel slowly loses stress over time (like a stretched rubber band slowly relaxing). Low-relaxation strand minimizes this.

Magnitude: 2-4% of initial stress (low-relaxation strand)
Much higher (8-12%) for stress-relieved (non-low-relaxation) strand
💡 Summary Table (Memorize for Interview!):
| Loss Type | When Happens | Typical % | Most Affected By |
|---|---|---|---|
| Elastic Shortening | Immediately | 4-7% | Concrete stress at transfer |
| Anchorage Draw-in | Immediately | 1-4% | Tendon LENGTH (shorter = worse) |
| Friction | Immediately | 3-10% | Tendon length + curvature |
| Creep | Over months | 4-6% | Sustained stress level |
| Shrinkage | Over months | 5-8% | Environment, member size |
| Relaxation | Ongoing | 2-4% | Stress level, strand type |
| TOTAL | | 20-30% | |
🔢 All Loss Formulas Together:
1. ES: Δfes = (Es/Ec) × fci × (Ap/Ac)
2. Anchorage: ΔPa = (Ap × Ep × ΔL)/L
3. Friction: Px = P0 × e^(-(Kx+μα))
4. Creep: Δfcr = n × fci × Ψ × ρp
5. Shrinkage: Δfsh = εsh × Es
6. Relaxation: Δfrel ≈ 0.02-0.04 × fpi (approximate)
Lesson 12: Load Balancing Method (Core Concept!)
The KEY concept that makes PT work - understand this deeply!

📖 The Big Idea Behind Load Balancing

In conventional RC, we let gravity load bend the slab, then add steel to RESIST that bending. In PT, we do something smarter: we use the tendon to APPLY an UPWARD force that CANCELS OUT part of the downward gravity load!

💡 Analogy: Carrying a Heavy Box
Method 1 (RC approach): Let box push down on your arms, then tense muscles to hold position.
Method 2 (PT approach): Have someone LIFT UP from below while you hold down. Net force on you is much less!

PT tendons act like the person lifting up from below.
THE KEY FORMULA (MEMORIZE!):

For a PARABOLIC tendon profile:
wb = 8 × Pe × e / L²

Where:
wb = Balanced load (upward force per meter) [kN/m]
Pe = Effective prestress force after losses [kN]
e = Drape (vertical distance between high and low points) [m]
L = Span length [m]
Worked Example:
PT one-way slab, span L = 10m
Tendon drape e = 150mm = 0.15m (high at support, low at midspan)
Effective prestress Pe = 1200 kN (per meter width of slab)
DL = 8 kPa, LL = 3 kPa

Calculate balanced load:
wb = 8 × 1200 × 0.15 / 10² = 1440 / 100 = 14.4 kN/m (or 14.4 kPa)

What does this mean?
Our tendons produce 14.4 kPa upward force!
DL = 8 kPa downward
Net unbalanced load = 14.4 - 8 = 6.4 kPa UPWARD (over-balanced!)
Or balance 100% DL + partial LL: 14.4 - 8 = 6.4 kPa left for LL capacity

If we want exact balance of DL only:
Required Pe = wb × L² / (8e) = 8 × 100 / (8 × 0.15) = 667 kN/m
So we could reduce prestress if we only want to balance DL
📌 Load Balancing Strategy Choices:
Option A: Balance 100% DL only
→ Slab sees only live load (good for parking, warehouses with heavy variable loads)
→ Deflection mainly from LL only

Option B: Balance DL + some portion of LL
→ Common choice: balance DL + 50-75% of LL
→ Very small net unbalanced load → minimal deflection
→ Good for offices, residential (consistent loading)

Option C: Over-balance
→ wb > total load → slab cambers upward initially
→ Risky: may cause problems if actual loads are less than assumed
💡 Why Parabolic Profile?
A parabolic tendon produces UNIFORM upward distributed load (just like reversed UDL!). This perfectly matches the uniform downward gravity load from slab self-weight and superimposed dead loads.

Other profiles produce different effects:
• Straight tendon → No transverse load, just end compression
• Harped (triangular) tendon → Concentrated upward force at kink point
• Parabolic tendon → Uniform upward load ← BEST for slabs!
🔢 Extended Formulas:
• Harped tendon (2-point): P = Wbal × L / (4e)
• Stresses at midspan (after balancing):
f_top = -P/A + Pec/I - M_unbal×(c_top)/I
f_bottom = -P/A - Pec/I + M_unbal×(c_bot)/I
• Where M_unbal = moment from (total load - balanced load)
Lesson 13: PT Slab Design Methodology
Complete step-by-step procedure for designing a PT slab

📖 Full Design Procedure (Your Interview Cheat Sheet)

STEP 1: Determine Geometry & Loads
• Panel dimensions (span lengths in both directions)
• Slab thickness (use L/40 to L/45 for two-way PT flat plate)
• Loads: Self-weight, SDL, LL, PT self-weight allowance
• Material properties: f'c (usually 35-45 MPa for PT), fy, fpu (strand)
STEP 2: Select Preliminary Thickness
Two-way flat plate: h ≥ L/40 to L/45
One-way system: h ≥ L/35 to L/40
Check deflection limits later (L/250 or L/360)
STEP 3: Define Tendon Profile
• High point at supports (near top of slab, maintain cover)
• Low point at midspan (near bottom of slab, maintain cover)
• Cover requirements: 25-40mm to CG of strand (check project specs)
• Eccentricity e = distance from CG of section to CG of strand
STEP 4: Calculate Required Prestress Force
Using load balancing: Pe = wb × L² / (8e)
Decide what percentage of load to balance (typically DL or DL+part LL)
STEP 5: Determine Number of Tendons
• Force per strand = effective stress × area of one strand
• 0.5" strand: Ap = 98.7 mm², fpe ≈ 1100 MPa (after losses)
• Force per strand ≈ 108-110 kN (typical)
• Number of tendons = Total Pe required / Force per tendon
STEP 6: Check Stresses at TRANSFER (Initial Stage)
ACI Limits (immediately after stressing):
• Compression: ≤ 0.6f'ci (f'ci = strength at transfer, usually ~25 MPa)
• Tension: ≤ 0.25√f'ci (with proper bonded reinforcement allowed)
Check at critical sections: Supports (top fiber) and Midspan (bottom fiber)
STEP 7: Check Stresses at SERVICE (Long-term Stage)
ACI Limits (after all losses, under full service load):
• Compression: ≤ 0.45f'c
• Tension: Decompression allowed (f_t ≥ 0) with proper bonded rebar
This is usually the GOVERNING CHECK!
STEP 8: Ultimate Strength (Flexural) Design
Treat PT section as RC section with known compression from PT
Add bonded reinforcement as needed for φMn ≥ Mu
Minimum bonded rebar: As = 0.001Ag (or per strip calculation)
STEP 9: Punching Shear Check (CRITICAL for Flat Plates!)
Critical section at d/2 from column face
Check: φVn ≥ Vu
Include PT vertical component contribution
If inadequate: add shear studs, drop panels, or column capitals
STEP 10: Detailing
• Anchorage zone reinforcement (bursting/spalling bars)
• Minimum bonded rebar placement
• Tendon spacing (usually 600-900mm c/c)
• Coordination with MEP openings
Quick Design Example Summary:
Panel: 8m × 8m, h = 220mm, f'c = 35 MPa, LL = 3 kPa
DL = 0.22×25 + 2 = 7.5 kPa
Choose: Balance 100% DL = 7.5 kPa
Drape e = 0.22 - 0.04(top) - 0.04(bot) - 0.013(strand dia/2) ≈ 127mm = 0.127m
Pe per meter = 7.5 × 8² / (8 × 0.127) = 472 kN/m
Strands per meter = 472/110 = 4.3 → use 5 strands/m @ 200mm c/c
Check stresses → OK → Check punching shear → OK → Done!
🔢 Essential Stress Check Formulas:
f = P/A ± Pe²/I ± M·y/I (combined stress formula)
At transfer: f_top,bottom using Pi (initial prestress)
At service: f_top,bottom using Pe (effective prestress) + service moments

• Good for large openings coordination

Module 2.3: PT Details & Practical Knowledge ▼
Lesson 16: PT Anchorage Details & Zone Reinforcement
Live end, dead end, bursting reinforcement - what happens at anchorages

📖 Types of Anchorages

LIVE END (Stressing End):
• Wedge anchor system (active anchorage)
• Hydraulic jack grips strand here, applies tension
• After stressing, wedges lock strand in place
• Components: Anchor casting, Wedges, Trumpet/Bell, Bearing plate
• Located at slab edge or pocket formed in slab

DEAD END (Non-Stressing End):
• Swaged anchor or barrel-and-wedge fixed anchor
• Strand is permanently locked here, no jacking
• Used when only one-end stressing is possible
• Or at intermediate anchorages (couplers)
💡 Anchorage Zone Forces (Critical Concept!):
When prestress force is transferred to concrete at anchorage, it creates:

1. Bursting Force:
Tendency for concrete to split apart BEHIND anchor (along tendon axis)
Counter with: Bursting reinforcement (hairpins/stirrups) behind anchor

2. Spalling Force:
Tendency for edge concrete to break off (perpendicular to tendon axis)
Counter with: Edge reinforcement, proper cover, confinement

Rule of thumb: Provide minimum 4-6 legs of T10/T12 hairpin reinforcement around each anchorage zone
Typical Live End Detail Sequence (from outside to inside):
1. Pocket former (removable after stressing) OR recess formed
2. Anchor casting (wedge plate assembly)
3. Bearing plate (distributes force to concrete)
4. Trumpet/bell (transitions from large bearing plate to small duct)
5. Duct/sheath (carries strand through concrete)
6. Bursting reinforcement (stirrups/hairpins around zone)
7. Strand (inside duct, extending into slab body)
🔢 Cover Requirements for PT:
• To duct/tendon: Usually 25-40mm (check project specs)
• To anchorage device: 25-40mm minimum
• For corrosion protection: More cover in aggressive environments
• For fire rating: Additional cover may be required (1hr, 2hr fire ratings)

Module 3.1: Column Design ▼
Lesson 17: Short Column Design
Designing axially loaded and eccentrically loaded columns

📖 Column Basics

Columns carry vertical loads from beams/slabs down to foundation. They can also carry moments from frame action.

Short vs Slender Columns:
SHORT COLUMN: kl/r ≤ 22 (approximately)
Strength governed by MATERIAL strength only (no extra moment from buckling)

SLENDER COLUMN: kl/r > 22
Must consider additional moments due to buckling (P-Delta effects)

Most building columns in typical frames are SHORT columns
Key Column Formulas:

Pure Axial Load (No Moment):
φPn = 0.65 × [0.85f'c(Ag - Ast) + fyAst]
Where: Ag = gross area, Ast = total steel area

With Moment (Interaction Required):
Use Interaction Diagram (P-M curve)
Each combination of Pu and Mu must fall INSIDE the curve

Minimum Reinforcement:
ρ_min = 0.01 (1%) to ensure ductility
Maximum Reinforcement:
ρ_max = 0.08 (8%) practical limit (code allows up to 0.08)
Quick Column Check Example:
Column: 500mm × 500mm, f'c = 40 MPa, fy = 500 MPa
Pu = 3000 kN, Mu_x = 80 kNm
Provide 8T25 bars (Ast = 3927 mm²)

Ag = 500 × 500 = 250,000 mm²
ρ = 3927/250000 = 0.0157 = 1.57% ✓ (between 1% and 8%)

Pure axial capacity (approximate):
φPn = 0.65 × [0.85×40×(250000-3927) + 500×3927]/1000
φPn = 0.65 × [8,366,484 + 1,963,500]/1000 = 6714 kN >> 3000 kN ✓

With small moment, still well within interaction diagram ✓
💡 Practical Column Design Tips:
• Keep ρ between 1.5% to 3% for economy and constructability
• Use minimum 4 bars for rectangular columns, 6 for circular
• Tie spacing: min(48 tie diameters, 16 bar diameters, column dimension)
• Corner bars MUST have lateral support from ties
• Every alternate bar (max 180mm spacing) needs tie support
🔢 Quick Reference:
• Tie size: T10 or T12 typical (min T10 for T32 bars and smaller)
• Tie spacing: Usually 150mm, 200mm, or 250mm c/c
• Clear cover to ties: 40mm (internal), 50mm (exposed)
Module 3.2: Foundation Design ▼
Lesson 18: Isolated Footing Design
Most common foundation type - step by step design

📖 Isolated Footing (Pad Footing)

Individual footing under each column. Most economical when columns are well-spaced and soil is reasonably good.

Design Steps:

Step 1: Determine Footing Size (Plan Dimension)
Based on Soil Bearing Capacity (SBC):
Area required = Pu / (SBC × safety factor) ... OR Service load approach
Size: Square (B×B) or Rectangular (B×L)

Step 2: Determine Depth (One-Way Shear Check)
Critical section at 'd' from column face
Vu = qnet × (B/2 - c/2 - d) × B (for square footing)
φVc ≥ Vu → find required d

Step 3: Check Punching Shear (Two-Way Shear)
Critical section at d/2 from column face (perimeter = 2(b+c+2d))
Vu = qnet × [B² - (c+d)²] (for square footing)
φVc(punching) ≥ Vu

Step 4: Flexural Reinforcement
Moment at column face: M = qnet × B × ((B-c)/2)² / 2
Design like wide beam: As = M / (φfy × (d - cover))
Distribute rebar uniformly in both directions (square) or calculate separately (rectangular)

Step 5: Detailing
Development length check, minimum reinforcement, cover (usually 50mm)
Example:
Column: 400×400mm, Pu = 2000 kN, SBC = 250 kPa
f'c = 30 MPa, fy = 500 MPa, γ = 1.5

Size: Area = 2000/(250/1.5) = 12 m² → Try 3.5m × 3.5m
Net pressure qu = Pu/Area = 2000/12.25 = 163.3 kPa

Depth (try d=500mm, D≈550mm):
One-way shear: Check OK (calculation omitted for brevity)
Punching shear: Check OK

Rebar: M = 163.3 × 3.5 × (1.55)² / 2 = 689 kNm
As required ≈ 2400 mm² → Use T20@200 c/c both ways ✓
💡 Dubai Foundation Considerations:
• Coastal Dubai: Sandy soils, SBC often 100-250 kPa
• May need deeper footings or piles for high-rise columns
• Groundwater table affects construction (dewatering needed)
• Sulfate/gypsum content in Sharjah inland soils → sulfate-resistant cement
🔢 Quick Formulas:
• Footing size from SBC: A = Pservice / SBC_allowable
• One-way shear: Vu = qu × B × (B-c)/2 - d (simplified)
• Punching perimeter: bo = 2(b1 + b2 + 2d) for rectangular column
• Punching capacity: φVc = 0.33√f'c × bo × d (simplified ACI)
Module 3.3: Shear Wall Basics ▼
Lesson 19: Shear Wall Fundamentals
Understanding shear walls for lateral load resistance

📖 What Are Shear Walls?

Vertical reinforced concrete elements designed to resist LATERAL loads (wind, earthquake). They act like deep cantilever beams fixed at base.

Types of Shear Walls:
• Rectangular walls: Simple planar wall (most common)
• Flanged walls: L-shaped, T-shaped, U-shaped (wall connected to perpendicular wall)
• Coupled walls: Two walls connected by coupling beams at each floor
• Core walls: Box-shaped (elevator/stairwell enclosure) - very efficient!
💡 Why Shear Walls in Dubai Buildings?
• Dubai is low-seismic (Zone 0-1), BUT...
• Tall buildings need stiffness against WIND loads
• Without shear walls, building would sway too much ( occupants feel uncomfortable)
• Shear walls provide drift control (limit lateral displacement)
• Core wall around elevator shaft is standard in almost every high-rise
Shear Wall Behavior Analogy:
Imagine holding a book vertically on table:
• Push from side → book tips over (OVERturning moment)
• Hold base firmly → book bends (like cantilever beam)
• Shear wall = firmly held cantilever resisting wind push

Resulting forces in wall:
• COMPRESSION on windward side edge
• TENSION on leeward side edge
• SHEAR throughout the wall cross-section
🔢 Basic Checks:
• Slenderness: hw/Lw (height/length) - slender walls behave differently
• Axial load + moment interaction (similar to column but wider)
• Shear capacity: φ(Vc + Vs) ≥ Vu
• Boundary elements: Confined zones at edges (heavy reinforcement)

🔢 Formula Cheat Sheet (Always Remember!)

STRENGTH OF MATERIALS

σ = P / A — Stress = Force / Area (units: MPa = N/mm²)
ε = δ / L — Strain = Elongation / Original Length (no units)
E = σ / ε — Young's Modulus (Steel: 200,000 MPa | Concrete: 25,000-35,000 MPa)
δ = PL / AE — Elongation of axially loaded member
σ = My / I — Bending stress at distance y from NA
τ = VQ / Ib — Shear stress at any point
I_rect = bh³/12 — MOI for rectangle
Z = I/y_max — Section Modulus

REINFORCED CONCRETE DESIGN (ACI 318)

U = 1.2D + 1.6L — Primary load combination (MOST USED!)
U = 1.4D — Dead load only
φ_flexure = 0.9 — Strength reduction factor for bending
φ_shear = 0.75 — Strength reduction factor for shear
φ_compression = 0.65 — For compression-controlled
Rn = Mu / (φbd²) — Required moment coefficient
ρ = As / (bd) — Reinforcement ratio
ρ_min = 1.4/fy — Minimum reinforcement ratio
Vc = 0.17√f'c × bw × d — Concrete shear capacity (simplified)
s = Av×fy×d / Vs — Stirrup spacing
Mmax_SSIMPLE_UDL = wL²/8 — Max moment for SS beam with UDL
Vmax_SSIMPLE_UDL = wL/2 — Max shear for SS beam with UDL
ld = (fyψtψeψs)/(25√f'c) × db — Development length (basic)

POST-TENSIONING (THE BIG ONES!)

Px = P₀ × e^(-(Kx + μα)) — FRICTION LOSS (MEMORIZE!)
wb = 8Pe/L² — LOAD BALANCING (parabolic tendon) (MEMORIZE!)
ΔP_anchor = (Ap × Ep × ΔL) / L — Anchorage seating loss
