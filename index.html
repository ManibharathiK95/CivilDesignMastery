Goal: Learn the language of engineers - Strength of Materials & RC Basics

L1: Stress, Strain & Material Properties

What is STRESS?
When you apply force on an object, internal resistance develops inside it. This internal force per unit area is called Stress.

🔢 FORMULA: σ = P / A σ = Stress (MPa or N/mm²)
P = Applied Force (N or kN)
A = Cross-sectional Area (mm² or m²)
💡 EXAMPLE 1: Calculate stress in column
A column carries 500 kN load.
Column size = 300mm × 300mm
Area A = 300 × 300 = 90,000 mm²
Stress σ = 500,000 N ÷ 90,000 mm² = 5.56 MPa

✅ This means every square mm of concrete carries 5.56 Newtons of force.

What is STRAIN?
Strain measures how much a material deforms (stretches or compresses) compared to its original length.

🔢 FORMULA: ε = δ / L ε = Strain (dimensionless, usually very small number like 0.001)
δ = Deformation/change in length (mm)
L = Original length (mm)
💡 EXAMPLE 2: Calculate strain in steel rod
Steel rod length = 2000mm (2 meters)
It stretches by 2mm under load
Strain ε = 2mm ÷ 2000mm = 0.001 (or 0.1%)

✅ Strain has NO units - it's just a ratio.

HOOKE'S LAW (Most Important Relationship)
Within elastic limit, Stress is directly proportional to Strain.

🔢 FORMULA: E = σ / ε OR σ = E × ε E = Young's Modulus (Modulus of Elasticity) - material stiffness
• Steel: E ≈ 200,000 MPa (200 GPa) - Very stiff
• Concrete: E ≈ 25,000-30,000 MPa (25-30 GPa) - Less stiff
• Aluminum: E ≈ 70,000 MPa (70 GPa)
💡 EXAMPLE 3: Using Hooke's Law
Concrete in compression: strain ε = 0.002
Concrete E = 25,000 MPa
Stress σ = E × ε = 25,000 × 0.002 = 50 MPa

⚠️ But typical concrete strength f'c = 25-40 MPa only!
So this concrete would CRUSH before reaching 50 MPa.
📌 THUMB RULES TO REMEMBER:
• Concrete compressive strength f'c = 25-40 MPa (normal buildings)
• Steel yield strength fy = 420 MPa (Grade 60) or 500 MPa (Grade 75)
• 1 MPa = 1 N/mm² = 1,000 kN/m²
• Typical building column stress: 8-15 MPa
• Typical beam bending stress: 10-20 MPa
💡 KEY CONCEPTS FOR INTERVIEW:
• Elastic Limit: If you remove load, material returns to original shape
• Yield Point: Permanent deformation starts (material doesn't fully recover)
• Ultimate Strength: Maximum stress material can take before breaking
• Ductile vs Brittle: Steel stretches before breaking (ductile). Concrete breaks suddenly (brittle). That's why we put steel IN concrete!
🔢 FORMULAS - MEMORIZE THESE 4 ALWAYS:
① σ = P/A (Stress formula)
② ε = δ/L (Strain formula)
③ E = σ/ε (Hooke's Law / Young's Modulus)
④ δ = PL/AE (Elongation formula - derived from above three)

L2: Bending Moment & Shear Force Diagrams

Why do we need SFD and BMD?
Before designing any beam, you MUST know where the maximum moment and maximum shear occur. These diagrams show exactly that.

SHEAR FORCE (V):
The force that tries to slide one part of beam relative to another part. Think of scissors cutting paper - that's shear action.

BENDING MOMENT (M):
The internal moment that tries to bend the beam. More moment = more bending = more stress at top and bottom fibers.

💡 EXAMPLE: Simply Supported Beam with UDL

Given data:
• Span L = 6 meters
• Uniformly Distributed Load w = 10 kN/m (dead + live load)
• Supports at both ends (simply supported)

Step 1: Find Reactions
Total load = w × L = 10 × 6 = 60 kN
Each support reaction = 60/2 = 30 kN ↑

Step 2: Maximum Shear Force
V_max occurs at supports = 30 kN
V at midspan = 0 (changes sign)

Step 3: Maximum Bending Moment
M_max occurs at midspan = wL²/8 = 10×36/8 = 45 kNm

✅ Design your beam for M = 45 kNm and V = 30 kN!
🔢 MUST-MEMORIZE FORMULAS:

SIMPLY SUPPORTED BEAM + UDL (most common):
• Reaction at each support: R = wL/2
• Max shear: V_max = wL/2 (at supports)
• Max moment: M_max = wL²/8 (at center)
• Moment at distance x: M = wx(L-x)/2

SIMPLY SUPPORTED + POINT LOAD P AT CENTER:
• Reaction: R = P/2 each side
• Max shear: V_max = P/2
• Max moment: M_max = PL/4 (at center)

CANTILEVER BEAM + UDL (fixed one end, free other end):
• Max shear: V_max = wL (at fixed end)
• Max moment: M_max = wL²/2 (at fixed end)

CANTILEVER + POINT LOAD P AT FREE END:
• Max shear: V_max = P (constant throughout)
• Max moment: M_max = PL (at fixed end)
📌 THUMB RULES:
• SFD jumps UP at upward reaction, DOWN at point load
• SFD slopes downward under UDL (slope = -w)
• Where V = 0 (crosses zero), M is MAXIMUM or MINIMUM
• For cantilever: worst case always at FIXED end
• For simply supported: worst case usually at MIDSPAN
💡 RELATIONSHIP BETWEEN SHEAR AND MOMENT:
• dV/dx = -w (slope of shear diagram = negative of load intensity)
• dM/dx = V (slope of moment diagram = shear value)
• This means: Moment is MAXIMUM where Shear = ZERO

Why interviewers ask this?
It shows you understand the FUNDAMENTALS, not just formulas!

L3: Bending Stress in Beams (Flexure Formula)

The Big Question: We know the bending moment M. Now what stress does this create in the beam?

Answer: Use the Flexure Formula!

🔢 MAIN FORMULA: σ = My / I
σ = Bending stress at distance y from neutral axis (MPa)
M = Bending moment (Nmm or kNm - be careful with units!)
y = Distance from neutral axis to where you want stress (mm)
I = Moment of Inertia of cross-section (mm⁴)
💡 COMPLETE EXAMPLE: Find max stress in rectangular beam

Given:
• Beam width b = 300 mm
• Total depth h = 600 mm
• Bending moment M = 50 kNm = 50×10⁶ Nmm

Step 1: Calculate I (Moment of Inertia)
For rectangle: I = bh³/12
I = 300 × (600)³ / 12 = 300 × 216,000,000 / 12
I = 5.4 × 10⁹ mm⁴

Step 2: Find y_max (distance to extreme fiber)
y_max = h/2 = 600/2 = 300 mm

Step 3: Calculate max stress
σ_max = My/I = (50×10⁶)(300) / (5.4×10⁹)
σ_max = 15×10⁹ / 5.4×10⁹ = 2.78 MPa

✅ This stress occurs at TOP and BOTTOM fibers (compression at top, tension at bottom)

SECTION MODULUS (Z) - Simplified Version:
Instead of calculating I and y separately, we use Z = I/y_max

🔢 SIMPLIFIED FORMULA: σ = M / Z

For rectangular section: Z = bh²/6
For our example: Z = 300×(600)²/6 = 300×360,000/6 = 18×10⁶ mm³
Then: σ = 50×10⁶ / 18×10⁶ = 2.78 MPa ✓ Same answer!
📌 SECTION MODULUS VALUES (MEMORIZE):
• Rectangle (b×h): Z = bh²/6
• Circle (diameter D): Z = πD³/32
• T-beam: Calculate I first, then Z = I/y_bottom (for tension side)
💡 WHY THIS MATTERS FOR RC DESIGN:
• Concrete is WEAK in tension (only 10% of its compressive strength)
• So we place STEEL REBAR at the bottom (where tension occurs)
• Steel takes all the tension; concrete takes all the compression
• This is why reinforced concrete works!

L4: Singly Reinforced Concrete Beam Design (ACI 318)

This is THE most important lesson for RC design interviews.
You will design beams like this 90% of the time.

THE DESIGN PHILOSOPHY (Limit State Method):

  • We INCREASE loads by factors (to be safe): 1.2×DL + 1.6×LL
  • We DECREASE capacity by factor φ (phi): φ = 0.9 for flexure
  • Requirement: φMn ≥ Mu (capacity must be greater than demand)
💡 COMPLETE BEAM DESIGN EXAMPLE:

GIVEN DATA:
• Span: 6m (simply supported)
• Dead Load (excluding self-weight): 15 kN/m
• Live Load: 20 kN/m
• f'c = 28 MPa (concrete strength)
• fy = 420 MPa (steel Grade 60)
• Assume beam width b = 300 mm

STEP 1: Calculate Factored Moment (Mu)
Self-weight of beam (assume 600mm deep): 0.3×0.6×25 = 4.5 kN/m
Total DL = 15 + 4.5 = 19.5 kN/m
Wu = 1.2(19.5) + 1.6(20) = 23.4 + 32 = 55.4 kN/m
Mu = WuL²/8 = 55.4×36/8 = 249.3 kNm

STEP 2: Assume effective depth d
Let total depth h = 600mm
Effective depth d = h - cover - stirrup - half bar
d = 600 - 40 - 10 - 12 = 538 mm ≈ 540 mm

STEP 3: Calculate required steel ratio ρ
Rn = Mu/(φbd²) = (249.3×10⁶)/(0.9×300×540²) = 3.18 MPa
ρ = (0.85f'c/fy)[1 - √(1 - 2Rn/(0.85f'c))]
ρ = (0.85×28/420)[1 - √(1 - 2×3.18/(0.85×28))]
ρ = 0.0567[1 - √(1 - 0.267)] = 0.0567[1 - 0.856] = 0.0082

STEP 4: Calculate required steel area As
As = ρ × b × d = 0.0082 × 300 × 540 = 1328 mm²

STEP 5: Select bars
Try 4#25 bars (25mm diameter): As provided = 4×491 = 1964 mm² > 1328 ✅
Or try 3#28: As = 3×616 = 1848 mm² > 1328 ✅

STEP 6: Check limits
ρ_min = 0.25√28/420 = 0.00315... OK (0.0082 > 0.00315) ✅
ρ_max = 0.75ρb = 0.021... OK (0.0082 < 0.021) ✅

✅ FINAL ANSWER: Use 4#25mm bars at bottom of beam!
🔢 ACI 318 FORMULAS - INTERVIEW READY:

Load Factors:
• U = 1.2D + 1.6L (most common)
• U = 1.4D (dead load only)

Strength Reduction Factors (φ):
• Flexure (tension controlled): φ = 0.90
• Shear and torsion: φ = 0.75
• Compression tied members: φ = 0.65

Reinforcement Limits:
• ρ_min = max(0.25√f'c/fy, 1.4/fy)
• ρ_max = 0.75ρb (ensures tension-controlled, ductile failure)

Development Length:
• ld = (fy/λ√f'c) × (ψtψeψs/25) × db
• For #25 bar, f'c=28, fy=420: ld ≈ 45db ≈ 1125mm
📌 PRACTICAL DESIGN TIPS:
• Typical beam width: 250-400mm (modular with wall/column sizes)
• Typical beam depth: span/10 to span/12 (for RC beams)
• Effective depth d ≈ h - 65mm (cover 40mm + stirrup 10mm + half bar ~15mm)
• Always check deflection control: L/depth ratio limits in ACI Table 9.3.1.1
• Never use less than 2 bars in beam (for stirrup holding)
💡 WHAT IF MY DESIGN FAILS CHECKS?
• If ρ > ρ_max: Increase beam depth OR add compression steel (doubly reinforced)
• If ρ < ρ_min: Provide minimum reinforcement anyway (for shrinkage/temperature)
• If bars don't fit in width: Increase beam width OR use smaller diameter more bars
• If deflection too much: Increase depth (deflection ∝ L²/d, so doubling d reduces deflection by 8x!)

L5: Shear Design in RC Beams

Why shear matters: Many beam failures happen due to shear, not flexure! Shear failure is SUDDEN and BRITTLE (no warning).

How shear works in concrete beams:

  • Concrete itself resists some shear (Vc)
  • If Vc is not enough, we add stirrups (Vs) to resist remaining shear
  • Total capacity: φ(Vc + Vs) ≥ Vu
🔢 ACI 318 SHEAR DESIGN FORMULAS:

Concrete shear capacity (simplified for members without axial force):
Vc = 0.17√f'c × bw × d (units: N, when f'c in MPa, dimensions in mm)

Required stirrup shear:
Vs = Vu/φ - Vc

Stirrup spacing calculation:
s = (Av × fy × d) / Vs
Where Av = area of both legs of stirrup (2-leg stirrup: Av = 2×π×φ²/4)
💡 EXAMPLE: Design shear reinforcement

Given: bw=300mm, d=540mm, f'c=28MPa, fy=420MPa, Vu=150kN

Step 1: Calculate Vc
Vc = 0.17 × √28 × 300 × 540 = 0.17 × 5.29 × 162,000 = 145,700 N = 145.7 kN

Step 2: Check if stirrups needed
φVc = 0.75 × 145.7 = 109.3 kN
Vu = 150 kN > 109.3 kN → YES, stirrups needed!

Step 3: Calculate Vs needed
Vs = Vu/φ - Vc = 150/0.75 - 145.7 = 200 - 145.7 = 54.3 kN

Step 4: Design stirrups
Use T10mm 2-legged stirrups: Av = 2 × 78.5 = 157 mm²
s = (157 × 420 × 540) / (54.3 × 1000) = 658 mm

Step 5: Apply maximum spacing limits
s_max = d/2 = 270mm (ACI limit)
Also s_max ≤ 300mm (absolute max)
Use s = 260mm c/c (or practically: 250mm c/c)

✅ Provide T10@250mm c/c stirrups throughout beam!
📌 SHEAR DESIGN RULES OF THUMB:
• First check: Is Vu ≤ φVc/2? If yes, MINIMUM stirrups only (code requirement)
• Minimum stirrups: T10@max spacing (usually d/2 or 300mm, whichever less)
• Critical section: At face of support (at distance d from support for simplicity)
• Stirrups are ALWAYS closed loops (not open) in beams
• Start stirrups at 50mm from face of support
• Common stirrup sizes: T8, T10, T12 (T10 most common)
💡 WHY SHEAR FAILURE IS DANGEROUS:
• Flexure failure: Steel yields first → cracks appear → warning before collapse
• Shear failure: Diagonal crack suddenly propagates → immediate collapse → NO WARNING
• That's why ACI uses lower φ for shear (0.75 vs 0.90 for flexure) - more conservative!
• NEVER ignore shear design even if "moment governs"

L6: One-Way Slab Design

What is One-Way Slab?
Slab spanning mainly in ONE direction (length/width ratio ≥ 2:1). Load transfers to two parallel beams/walls.

💡 EXAMPLE: Design a one-way slab

Given:
• Clear span = 4m (between beams)
• Live load LL = 3 kPa (residential)
• Floor finish = 1.5 kPa
• f'c = 28 MPa, fy = 420 MPa

Step 1: Assume slab thickness
ACI minimum for one-way slab (simply supported): L/20 = 4000/20 = 200mm
Use h = 200mm, d = 200 - 25 (cover) - 6 (half bar) ≈ 170mm

Step 2: Calculate loads per m width
Self-weight = 0.2m × 25 kN/m³ = 5.0 kN/m²
Total DL = 5.0 + 1.5 = 6.5 kN/m²
Wu = 1.2(6.5) + 1.6(3) = 7.8 + 4.8 = 12.6 kN/m (per meter width)

Step 3: Calculate moment (per 1m strip)
Mu = WL²/8 = 12.6 × (4)² / 8 = 25.2 kNm/m

Step 4: Calculate main reinforcement
Rn = Mu/(φbd²) = (25.2×10⁶)/(0.9×1000×170²) = 0.969 MPa
ρ = calculate using formula ≈ 0.0029
As = ρbd = 0.0029 × 1000 × 170 = 493 mm²/m
Use T12@200 c/c: As = 566 mm²/m > 493 ✅

Step 5: Temperature/shrinkage reinforcement (perpendicular direction)
As_min = 0.0018 × b × h = 0.0018 × 1000 × 200 = 360 mm²/m
Use T10@200 c/c: As = 393 mm²/m > 360 ✅

✅ MAIN STEEL: T12@200 c/c (bottom, short direction)
✅ TEMP STEEL: T10@200 c/c (top, long direction)
🔢 SLAB DESIGN QUICK REFERENCE:

Minimum thickness (ACI Table 9.3.1.1):
• Simply supported one-way: L/20
• One end continuous: L/24
• Both ends continuous: L/28
• Cantilever: L/10

Min temperature reinforcement (Grade 60):
As = 0.0018 × Ag (Ag = b×h)

Max spacing of rebar:
• Main rebar: min(3h, 450mm)
• Temp rebar: min(5h, 450mm)
📌 SLAB DESIGN VS BEAM DESIGN - DIFFERENCE:
• Slab designed as 1-meter WIDE STRIP (b = 1000mm always)
• Slab thickness governed by DEFLECTION (span/depth ratio), not moment
• Temperature steel ALWAYS required in perpendicular direction
• Slab cover: 20-25mm (less than beams because no stirrups)
• Slab main bar at bottom (positive moment), top bars over supports (negative moment)

L7: Introduction to Post-Tensioning

WHAT IS POST-TENSIONING (PT)?

Post-Tensioning is a technique where we apply compressive force to concrete AFTER it has hardened and gained strength. We do this using high-strength steel strands (tendons) that are stretched and anchored against the concrete.

WHY USE PT? (The Benefits)

  • Longer spans: PT slabs can span 10-15m easily (RC slabs struggle beyond 6-8m)
  • Thinner sections: PT slab thickness = L/35 to L/45 (RC needs L/20 to L/28)
  • Less cracking: Compression keeps cracks closed under service loads
  • Better deflection control: Tendon profile can be shaped to counteract deflection
  • Material savings: Less concrete and often less total steel
📌 COMPONENTS YOU MUST KNOW:

1. STRAND (Tendon):
• Typically 0.5 inch (12.7mm) diameter 7-wire low-relaxation strand
• Grade 270: Ultimate strength fpu = 1860 MPa (270 ksi)
• Yield strength fpy ≈ 0.9 fpu ≈ 1670 MPa
• Cross-sectional area Ap = 93 mm² (per strand)

2. ANCHORAGE (The "Anchor"):
• Live End (Stressing End): Where hydraulic jack attaches. Has wedge plate, trumpet, and bursting reinforcement. After stressing, wedges grip the strand and hold force.
• Dead End (Fixed End): Other end of tendon. Swaged anchor prevents pull-out. No stressing happens here.

3. DUCT / SHEATHING:
• Plastic or galvanized steel tube that houses strand before grouting
• Allows strand to move freely during stressing operation
• Diameter typically 20-25mm for single strands

4. GROUT:
• Cementitious mixture injected into duct AFTER stressing
• Protects strand from corrosion
• Bonds strand to concrete (provides permanent lock)
💡 EXAMPLE: Calculate initial prestressing force

Problem: A PT beam has 6 strands of 0.5" diameter. Jacking stress = 0.75 fpu.

Solution:
fpu = 1860 MPa (given for Grade 270 strand)
Jacking stress fpj = 0.75 × 1860 = 1395 MPa
Area per strand = 93 mm²
Total area Ap = 6 × 93 = 558 mm²
Initial force Pi = Ap × fpj = 558 × 1395 = 778,310 N = 778 kN

✅ This 778 kN force will compress the beam!
💡 PRE-TENSIONING vs POST-TENSIONING:

PRE-TENSIONING:
• Strands tensioned BEFORE concrete is poured
• Used in PRECAST factories (hollow core slabs, bridge girders)
• Bond between strand and concrete transfers force (no anchors needed)
• Limited to factory production

POST-TENSIONING:
• Strands tensioned AFTER concrete hardens (strength reached)
• Used in CAST-IN-SITU construction (buildings, parking structures, water tanks)
• Anchors at ends transfer force to concrete
• Can be done ON SITE - this is what Dubai buildings use!
🔢 BASIC PT FORMULAS - START HERE:

① P = Ap × fpj (Initial jacking force)
② Pe = P - losses (Effective force after all losses, typically 70-80% of P)
③ fpe = Pe/Ap (Effective stress in strand after losses, typically 1000-1100 MPa)
④ Eccentricity e = distance from centroid of section to centroid of tendon

L8: Losses in Post-Tensioning (INTERVIEW CRITICAL!)

⚠️ WARNING: Interviewers WILL ask about losses. This is #1 PT question.

When we apply prestress force, we don't get to keep all of it. Some force is lost due to various reasons. Understanding these losses separates PT specialists from general engineers.

💡 TWO CATEGORIES OF LOSSES:

A) INSTANTANEOUS LOSSES (happen during stressing, within minutes):
1. Anchorage Seating (Wedge Draw-in)
2. Friction Loss (Elastic shortening during sequential stressing)
3. Elastic Shortening of Concrete

B) TIME-DEPENDENT LOSSES (happen over months/years):
4. Creep of Concrete
5. Shrinkage of Concrete
6. Relaxation of Strand

DETAILED EXPLANATION OF EACH LOSS:

📌 LOSS 1: ANCHORAGE SEATING (WEDGE DRAW-IN)

What happens: When the hydraulic jack releases, the anchorage wedges seat (settle) into the anchor casting. This pulls the strand back slightly.

Typical value: Wedge draw-in ΔL = 6mm (1/4 inch) for most systems

Formula: ΔP_anchor = (Ap × Ep × ΔL) / L

Key insight: SHORT tendons lose MORE percentage-wise than long tendons!
Example: 5m tendon loses much higher % than 30m tendon for same 6mm draw-in.
💡 Example: Calculate anchorage loss

Given: Strand Ap = 93 mm², Ep = 195,000 MPa, ΔL = 6mm, Tendon length L = 10m = 10,000mm

ΔP = (93 × 195,000 × 6) / 10,000 = 108,810 N = 108.8 kN loss

If initial Pi per strand = ~130kN (1395MPa × 93mm²), Loss % = 108.8/130 = 84%??
⚠️ Wait - that seems too high because tendon is short (10m). For longer tendons (20m+), this becomes small percentage.
📌 LOSS 2: FRICTION LOSS (Most complex loss)

Two components work together:

a) Wobble friction (K):
Even in a "straight" duct, the duct isn't perfectly straight. Small deviations cause friction.
K coefficient = 0.001 to 0.003 per meter (typical K = 0.002/m for bonded PT)

b) Curvature friction (μ):
When tendon curves (parabolic profile), friction increases dramatically.
μ coefficient = 0.05 to 0.30 (typical μ = 0.20 for sheathed strand)

THE BIG FORMULA:
Px = P₀ × e^(-(Kx + μα))
Where: Px = force at distance x from jacking end
P₀ = jacking force at start
x = distance along tendon (meters)
α = cumulative angular change (radians)
💡 Example: Calculate friction loss

Given: P₀ = 130 kN (jacking), K = 0.002/m, μ = 0.20
Tendon: Span 12m, parabolic profile, total angle change α = 0.08 radians (~4.6°)
Check force at far end (x = 12m):

Exponent = -(Kx + μα) = -(0.002×12 + 0.20×0.08) = -(0.024 + 0.016) = -0.04
e^(-0.04) = 0.9608
Px = 130 × 0.9608 = 124.9 kN

Loss = 130 - 124.9 = 5.1 kN = 3.9% loss

✅ For a 12m span, friction loss is about 4%. Acceptable.
📌 LOSS 3: ELASTIC SHORTENING

What happens: When you stress tendons, they compress the concrete. This compression causes the concrete to shorten elastically. Since the strand is bonded to concrete (or will be grouted), the strand also shortens = loss of stress.

Magnitude: About 1-3% of initial stress
Depends on: P/A ratio, concrete modulus, number of tendons stressed sequentially
📌 LOSS 4: CREEP (Time-dependent)

What happens: Under sustained compressive stress, concrete slowly deforms over time (like slow-motion squishing). This additional deformation causes more strand shortening = more loss.

Magnitude: About 4-6% of initial stress
Factors increasing creep: High sustained stress, young concrete at stressing, humid environment
📌 LOSS 5: SHRINKAGE (Time-dependent)

What happens: As concrete dries and loses water volume, it shrinks. This shrinkage shortens the member, which shortens the embedded strand.

Magnitude: About 5-6% of initial stress
Formula: Δfsh = εsh × Es
Where εsh = shrinkage strain (typically 400-800 × 10⁻⁶ for 3+ months)
📌 LOSS 6: RELAXATION (Time-dependent)

What happens: If you hold a steel strand at constant strain (stretched and held), the stress in the steel slowly decreases over time. This property is called "relaxation". Low-relaxation strand (standard today) has less relaxation.

Magnitude: About 2-4% of initial stress for low-relaxation strand
🔢 SUMMARY TABLE - MEMORIZE FOR INTERVIEW:

| Loss Type | Instantaneous/Time | Approx % | Key Factor |
|-----------|-------------------|----------|------------|
| Anchorage Seating | Instantaneous | 1-5% | Shorter tendons lose more % |
| Friction | Instantaneous | 3-10% | Longer tendons + more curvature = more loss |
| Elastic Shortening | Instantaneous | 1-3% | Depends on P/A ratio |
| Creep | Time-dependent | 4-6% | Higher stress = more creep |
| Shrinkage | Time-dependent | 5-6% | Depends on environment |
| Relaxation | Time-dependent | 2-4% | Low-relaxation strand = less |
| TOTAL | - | 20-30% | - |
💡 HOW TO REMEMBER ALL 6 LOSSES (Memory Trick):

Use acronym: "A-F-E-C-S-R" pronounced "A-fecer"
• Anchorage Seating
• Friction
• Elastic Shortening
• Creep
• Shrinkage
• Relaxation

Group them: AFE (instantaneous) + CSR (time-dependent)

L9: Load Balancing Method (Core PT Concept)

⭐ THIS IS THE MOST IMPORTANT CONCEPT IN PT DESIGN. UNDERSTAND THIS AND YOU UNDERSTAND PT.

THE BASIC IDEA:

When you have a parabolic-shaped tendon in a slab and you stretch it, the tendon wants to straighten out. But it's embedded in concrete, so it can't straighten. Instead, it pushes UPWARD on the concrete along its length. This upward force can BALANCE (cancel out) the downward gravity load!

🔢 THE LOAD BALANCING FORMULA:

w_balanced = 8 × P × e / L²

Where:
• w_balanced = Upward equivalent distributed load from tendon (kN/m or kPa)
• P = Effective prestress force after losses (kN)
• e = Drape of tendon (vertical distance from high point to low point) (m)
• L = Span length (m)

For a SINGLE span parabolic tendon:
The tendon shape creates a uniformly distributed upward load = 8Pe/L²
💡 COMPLETE EXAMPLE: Load balancing calculation

Given:
• Span L = 10 meters
• Effective prestress force P = 1200 kN (after losses)
• Tendon profile: Parabolic, high at supports, low at midspan
• Drape (eccentricity) e = 150 mm = 0.15 m
• Dead load DL = 8 kN/m (slab self-weight + finishes)
• Live load LL = 4 kN/m

Step 1: Calculate balanced load
w_b = 8 × P × e / L²
w_b = 8 × 1200 × 0.15 / (10)²
w_b = 1440 / 100 = 14.4 kN/m ← This is the UPWARD force!

Step 2: Compare with actual loads
Total downward load = DL + LL = 8 + 4 = 12 kN/m
Balanced load = 14.4 kN/m (UPWARD)

We're balancing MORE than total load - slight uplift at transfer stage (acceptable if within stress limits)

✅ Result: Net effect is slight uplift. Slab will have very low net stress under full service loads.
📌 WHY LOAD BALANCING IS SO POWERFUL:

1. Simplifies analysis: Instead of complex indeterminate PT analysis, you just analyze for UNBALANCED load only!

2. Intuitive understanding: You can visualize: "My tendon cancels out the dead weight, so my slab only feels live load."

3. Deflection control: If you balance 100% of DL, deflection under DL = ZERO theoretically!

4. Optimization tool: Adjust P or e to get desired balance percentage
💡 PRACTICAL DESIGN APPROACH USING LOAD BALANCING:

Step 1: Choose target balance ratio (typically 65-85% of total service load)
Step 2: Calculate required w_balanced = ratio × (DL + LL)
Step 3: Assume reasonable drape e (based on slab thickness and cover requirements)
Step 4: Calculate required P = w_b × L² / (8e)
Step 5: Determine number of strands: n = P / (Ap × fpe)
Step 6: Check stresses at transfer and service stages (next lesson!)

L10: PT Slab Stress Checks (ACI 18.4 Requirements)

After designing tendon force and profile, you MUST verify concrete stresses are within allowable limits. This is non-negotiable.

WHEN DO WE CHECK STRESSES?

  • At Transfer (Initial Stage): Right after stressing, when prestress is highest but concrete strength is lowest (f'ci)
  • At Service (Long-term Stage): After all losses, under full service loads (DL + LL)
🔢 ACI 318 STRESS LIMITS (MEMORIZE!):

STAGE 1: AT TRANSFER (when jacking happens)
Concrete strength = f'ci (initial strength, typically 0.7-0.85 of design f'c)

• Top fiber (compression, from prestress eccentricity): ≤ 0.25√f'ci
• Bottom fiber (TENSION allowed here! Special for PT): ≤ 0.6√f'ci

STAGE 2: AT SERVICE (long-term, all losses occurred)
Concrete strength = f'c (design 28-day strength)

• Top fiber (compression): ≤ 0.45√f'c
• Bottom fiber (decompression or slight tension with proper bonded rebar)

STAGE 3: FACTORED LOADS (ultimate strength check)
• Treat as RC member: φMn ≥ Mu (flexural strength check)
• Add rebar as needed for ultimate strength
💡 EXAMPLE: Stress check concept

Given: f'ci = 25 MPa at transfer, f'c = 35 MPa at service

Transfer Stage Limits:
Allowable compression = 0.25√25 = 0.25×5 = 1.25 MPa (top fiber)
Allowable tension = 0.6√25 = 0.6×5 = 3.0 MPa (bottom fiber)

Service Stage Limits:
Allowable compression = 0.45√35 = 0.45×5.92 = 2.66 MPa (top fiber)
Allowable tension at bottom = decompression (≤ 0) or small value with bonded rebar

✅ If your calculated stresses are within these limits → PASS. If not → adjust design.
📌 PRACTICAL STRESS CHECK GUIDELINES:

• If tension exceeds limit at transfer: Add bonded reinforcement in that zone
• If compression exceeds limit: Reduce prestress force or eccentricity
• Service stage usually controls design (more critical than transfer)
• Always check BOTH stages - don't assume one controls
• Use spreadsheet or software (SAFE) for accurate stress calculations
💡 WHY IS TENSION ALLOWED IN PT BUT NOT IN RC?

In ordinary RC, we don't allow tension because it would crack immediately.
In PT, even though theoretical tension exists, the PRESTRESS keeps cracks CLOSED.
The concrete is in overall compression - the "tension" is just reduction of compression at that fiber, not true cracking tension.

But you MUST provide minimum bonded rebar (ACI requirements) wherever calculated tension exceeds allowable limits.

L11: Punching Shear in Flat Plates (Critical Check!)

WHY THIS IS CRITICAL:
In flat plate systems (no beams, just columns and slab), the #1 failure mode is punching shear - the column literally punches through the slab. This failure is SUDDEN and CATASTROPHIC.

🔢 PUNCHING SHEAR CHECK PROCEDURE (ACI 22.6):

Step 1: Define critical section
• Located at d/2 from face of column (d = effective depth of slab)
• Perimeter b₀ = perimeter of this critical section

Step 2: Calculate factored shear force Vu
• Vu = total load on tributary area minus any direct column load reduction

Step 3: Calculate concrete shear capacity φVc
• φ = 0.75 (shear reduction factor)
• Vc depends on b₀, f'c, and geometry

Step 4: Compare: φVc ≥ Vu ?
• If YES: OK, no shear reinforcement needed
• If NO: Need shear studs, drop panel, column capital, or thicker slab
💡 EXAMPLE: Punching shear check for interior column

Given:
• Column: 550mm × 550mm square (interior)
• Slab: 220mm thick, effective depth d = 190mm
• Factored shear force Vu = 450 kN
• f'c = 35 MPa

Step 1: Critical perimeter b₀
Distance from column face = d/2 = 190/2 = 95mm
Critical section dimension = 550 + 95 + 95 = 740mm (each side)
b₀ = 4 × 740 = 2960 mm

Step 2: Calculate Vc (simplified)
Vc ≈ 0.17 × √f'c × b₀ × d = 0.17 × 5.92 × 2960 × 190 = 566 kN

Step 3: Apply φ factor
φVc = 0.75 × 566 = 424.5 kN

Step 4: Check
Vu = 450 kN > φVc = 424.5 kN ❌ FAIL by 25.5 kN (6%)

⚠️ Action required: Add shear stud rails around column (common solution)
📌 PUNCHING SHEAR TIPS FROM SITE EXPERIENCE:

• Openings near columns are DANGEROUS! They reduce b₀ significantly.
• Edge columns are weaker than interior columns (3-sided vs 4-sided perimeter).
• Shear stud rails are the most common solution in Dubai PT buildings.
💡 COMMON SOLUTIONS WHEN PUNCHING SHEAR FAILS:

1. Shear Stud Rails (most common for PT slabs) - Vertical studs welded to base rail, installed before pouring
2. Drop Panel - Local thickening of slab around column
3. Column Capital - Flared column head (architectural impact)
4. Increase Overall Slab Thickness - Simplest but heaviest option

L12: Column Design Fundamentals

As a PT designer, you focus on slabs. BUT to get hired by general consultancies, you MUST know column design too.

🔢 SHORT COLUMN DESIGN (Axial Load Dominated):

Design Formula (ACI 22.4):
φPn = 0.65 × [0.85f'c(Ag - As) + fyAs] ≥ Pu

Where:
• φ = 0.65 for tied columns (0.75 for spiral columns)
• Ag = gross area of column (mm²)
• As = total longitudinal steel area (mm²)
• Pu = factored axial load (N)
💡 EXAMPLE: Design a short column

Given:
• Pu = 5000 kN (factored axial load)
• Column size: 550mm × 550mm (assume preliminary)
• f'c = 40 MPa, fy = 500 MPa
• Assume 2% steel ratio

Step 1: Calculate areas
Ag = 550 × 550 = 302,500 mm²
As = 0.02 × 302,500 = 6,050 mm²

Step 2: Calculate capacity
φPn = 0.65 × [0.85×40×(302,500-6,050) + 500×6,050]
φPn = 0.65 × [9,973,000 + 3,025,000] = 8,448,700 N = 8,449 kN

8,449 kN > 5,000 kN ✅ OK - Column has good capacity margin
📌 COLUMN DESIGN REQUIREMENTS (ACI 318):

Reinforcement Limits:
• Minimum ρ = 0.01 (1% of Ag)
• Maximum ρ = 0.08 (8% of Ag) - practical max is 4%
• Min bars: 4 for rectangular, 6 for circular

Tie Requirements:
• Tie size: min #10 for bars up to #32
• Spacing: min of (16×long.bar dia, 48×tie dia, col dimension)
• Clear Cover: 40mm (interior), 50mm (exposed)

L13: Foundation Design - Isolated Footings

Foundations transfer building loads to ground. Get this wrong and the whole building settles/cracks/tilts.

🔢 FOOTING DESIGN STEPS:

Step 1: Determine service loads
Column load + footing self-weight estimate

Step 2: Size footing plan dimension
Area = (DL + LL) / SBC (Safe Bearing Capacity)

Step 3: Calculate net upward pressure
qnet = Pu / (L×B) (factored pressure for design)

Step 4: Check ONE-WAY SHEAR
Critical at d from face of column
φVc = 0.17√f'c × B × d ≥ Vu

Step 5: Check PUNCHING SHEAR
Similar to slab punching shear check

Step 6: Design flexural reinforcement
Moment at face of column, design as wide beam

Step 7: Check development length
💡 EXAMPLE: Design isolated footing

Given:
• Column: 400mm × 400mm
• Service load: DL = 800 kN, LL = 600 kN
• SBC (soil bearing capacity) = 200 kPa
• f'c = 30 MPa, fy = 450 MPa

Size footing:
Total load = 1400 kN + 10% self-weight = 1540 kN
Area = 1540/200 = 7.7 m² → Use 2.8m × 2.8m

✅ Footing 2.8m × 2.8m × 500mm thick with calculated bottom rebar
📌 FOUNDATION DESIGN TIPS:

• Always get soil report from geotechnical engineer before designing!
• Dubai coastal: Sandy soils, SBC 100-250 kPa typical
• Sharjah inland: May encounter harder layers, possible gypsum (sulfate attack risk!)
• Minimum footing thickness: 200-300mm
• Clear cover: 50-75mm (aggressive ground needs more)

L14: Shear Wall Design Basics

Shear walls are vertical elements that resist lateral forces (wind, earthquake). In Dubai high-rises, shear walls are essential for stability.

💡 TYPES OF SHEAR WALLS:

1. Rectangular Shear Wall: Simple planar wall, easiest to design
2. Flanged Wall (L-shape, T-shape, U-shape): Wall connected to perpendicular wall
3. Coupled Shear Walls: Two walls connected by coupling beams
4. Core Wall: Box-shaped walls around elevator/stair shafts
📌 SHEAR WALL DESIGN BASICS (Wind Controlled - Dubai Case):

Flexural Design:
• Wall acts as vertical cantilever
• Reinforcement: Vertical bars at edges (boundary elements) + distributed bars in web

Shear Design:
• Base shear from wind analysis
• Horizontal reinforcement ratio: typically 0.25-0.5%
• Vertical reinforcement: minimum 0.25%
💡 WHY SHEAR WALLS MATTER FOR YOUR CAREER:

• PT designers focus on slabs, but employers want WELL-ROUNDED engineers
• Understanding lateral loads makes you better at slab design too
• During site visits, seeing shear wall construction helps your overall engineering judgment
