📘 Module 1.1: Strength of Materials 0/4
○ Lesson 1: Stress & Strain Basics

🎯 What You Will Learn

  • What is Stress and why engineers calculate it
  • What is Strain and how materials deform
  • Hooke's Law - the relationship between stress and strain
  • Elastic Modulus (Young's Modulus) - material stiffness

📖 Theory

STRESS (σ): Force per unit area. When you apply force on a material, internal resistance develops. This internal force per area is called Stress.

σ = P / A

Where:
σ = Stress (MPa or N/mm²)
P = Applied Force (Newton or kN)
A = Cross-sectional Area (mm²)

STRAIN (ε): Deformation per unit length. It tells us how much a material stretches or compresses relative to its original size.

ε = δ / L

Where:
ε = Strain (unitless, often shown as % or microstrain)
δ = Change in length (mm)
L = Original length (mm)

HOOKE'S LAW: Within elastic limit, stress is directly proportional to strain. This means if you double the load, deformation also doubles.

E = σ / ε OR σ = E × ε

Where:
E = Young's Modulus / Elastic Modulus (MPa or GPa)
For Steel: E ≈ 200,000 MPa (200 GPa)
For Concrete: E ≈ 25,000-35,000 MPa
Typical value for concrete: Ec = 4700√f'c (MPa)
💡 Example Problem

A steel rod of diameter 20mm and length 2m carries a tensile load of 50 kN. Find:

  1. Stress in the rod
  2. If E = 200 GPa, find elongation
  3. Strain value

Solution:

Area A = π×d²/4 = π×20²/4 = 314 mm²

Stress σ = P/A = 50,000/314 = 159.2 MPa

Strain ε = σ/E = 159.2/200,000 = 0.000796 (or 0.0796%)

Elongation δ = ε×L = 0.000796×2000 = 1.59 mm

📌 THUMB RULES:
  • Steel can handle ~250 MPa stress safely (yield ~400-500 MPa)
  • Concrete handles only ~0.45f'c in bending tension zone (cracks!)
  • Concrete handles compression well (~0.85f'c max)
  • Poisson's ratio ν ≈ 0.3 for steel, 0.15-0.2 for concrete
💡 KEY CONCEPTS TO REMEMBER:
  • Stress has units (MPa), Strain has NO units (ratio)
  • Steeper slope on stress-strain curve = Stiffer material
  • Beyond elastic limit = permanent deformation (yielding)
  • In PT design, we PRE-STRESS concrete to counteract future tensile stresses
🔢 FORMULAS TO REMEMBER ALWAYS:

1. σ = P / A (Axial Stress)
2. ε = δ / L (Strain)
3. E = σ / ε (Hooke's Law)
4. δ = PL / AE (Elongation formula)
5. Ec = 4700√f'c (Concrete Elastic Modulus in MPa)
○ Lesson 2: Bending Moment & Shear Force Diagrams

🎯 What You Will Learn

  • What causes Bending Moment in beams
  • What is Shear Force
  • How to draw SFD (Shear Force Diagram)
  • How to draw BMD (Bending Moment Diagram)
  • Relationship between Load, Shear, and Moment

📖 Theory

SHEAR FORCE (V): The algebraic sum of all vertical forces acting on either side of a beam section. It represents the tendency of one part of the beam to slide past another.

BENDING MOMENT (M): The algebraic sum of moments about a section due to forces on either side. It represents the tendency of the beam to bend.

SIGN CONVENTION (Important!):

SHEAR FORCE:
• Upward force on LEFT side = POSITIVE (+)
• Downward force on RIGHT side = POSITIVE (+)

BENDING MOMENT:
• Sagging moment (smile shape 😊) = POSITIVE (+) - Tension at bottom
• Hogging moment (frown shape ☹️) = NEGATIVE (-) - Tension at top
RELATIONSHIPS (CRITICAL FOR EXAMS):

• dV/dx = w (Rate of change of shear = distributed load)
• dM/dx = V (Rate of change of moment = shear force)
• Maximum moment occurs where V = 0 (shear crosses zero)
💡 Example: Simply Supported Beam with UDL

Problem: A simply supported beam of span L = 6m carries UDL of w = 20 kN/m over entire span.

Find: Reactions, Max Shear, Max Moment, Draw SFD & BMD

Solution:

  1. Reactions: By symmetry, RA = RB = wL/2 = 20×6/2 = 60 kN ↑
  2. Shear Force:
    3.    • At A (just right): V = +60 kN    • At midspan: V = 60 - 20×3 = 0 kN ← Max moment here!    • At B (just left): V = -60 kN
  3. Max Shear: |V|max = 60 kN (at supports)
  4. Max Moment: At center where V=0
    5.    Mmax = wL²/8 = 20×36/8 = 90 kNm

Shape of diagrams:

• SFD: Linear from +60 to -60 (triangular shape)

• BMD: Parabolic curve, max 90 at center, zero at supports

📌 THUMB RULES FOR COMMON CASES:
  • Simply supported + UDL: Mmax = wL²/8, Vmax = wL/2
  • Simply supported + Point load P at center: Mmax = PL/4, Vmax = P/2
  • Cantilever + UDL: Mmax = wL²/2 (at fixed end), Vmax = wL
  • Cantilever + Point load P at free end: Mmax = PL (at fixed), Vmax = P
  • Fixed beam + UDL: Mmax = wL²/12 (at supports), Mcenter = wL²/24
💡 KEY CONCEPTS:
  • SFD shows where shear is maximum (design stirrups here!)
  • BMD shows where moment is maximum (design main reinforcement here!)
  • Point loads cause JUMPS in SFD, linear changes in BMD
  • UDL causes LINEAR SFD, PARABOLIC BMD
  • Moment is always ZERO at simple supports and free ends
🔢 FORMULAS TO REMEMBER ALWAYS:

1. Simply Supported + UDL: M = wL²/8, V = wL/2
2. Simply Supported + Central Point Load: M = PL/4, V = P/2
3. Cantilever + UDL: M = wL²/2, V = wL
4. Cantilever + End Point Load: M = PL, V = P
5. Fixed-Fixed + UDL: Msupport = wL²/12, Mcenter = wL²/24
6. dM/dx = V (moment slope equals shear)
○ Lesson 3: Bending Stresses in Beams

🎯 What You Will Learn

  • How bending creates stress distribution across beam depth
  • The Flexure Formula (most important formula!)
  • Section Modulus - what it means practically
  • Moment of Inertia calculation for rectangular sections
  • Why beams are deeper than they are wide

📖 Theory

When a beam bends, one side stretches (tension) and other side compresses (compression). There's a line in between called Neutral Axis where stress = 0.

FLEXURE FORMULA (THE BIG ONE!):

σ = My / I OR σ = M / Z

Where:
σ = Bending stress at distance y from NA (MPa)
M = Bending moment at section (Nmm or kNm)
y = Distance from Neutral Axis (mm)
I = Moment of Inertia about NA (mm⁴)
Z = Section Modulus = I/y_max (mm³)
FOR RECTANGULAR SECTION (b × h):

I = bh³ / 12
Z = bh² / 6

Where:
b = width of beam (mm)
h = total depth of beam (mm)
Note: Use effective depth d for RC design (not total h)
💡 Example Problem

A rectangular beam 300mm wide × 600mm deep carries a maximum moment of 80 kNm.

Find: Maximum bending stress

Solution:

I = bh³/12 = 300 × 600³ / 12 = 5.4 × 10⁹ mm⁴

y_max = h/2 = 300 mm (distance from center to extreme fiber)

Z = I/y_max = 5.4×10⁹ / 300 = 18 × 10⁶ mm³ = 18×10⁶ mm³

σ_max = M/Z = (80×10⁶ Nmm) / (18×10⁶ mm³) = 4.44 MPa

(This is low because it's plain concrete - we need steel reinforcement!)

📌 THUMB RULES:
  • Maximum stress occurs at TOP and BOTTOM fibers (farthest from NA)
  • Stress is ZERO at neutral axis (center for symmetric sections)
  • Doubling beam depth increases strength by 4 times! (h² in Z)
  • Doubling width only doubles strength
  • This is WHY beams are deeper than wide!
  • T-beams are efficient - more material away from NA
💡 KEY CONCEPTS:
  • Neutral Axis passes through centroid of cross-section
  • For RC beams: NA location depends on amount of steel (not at center!)
  • Compression stress at top, Tension stress at bottom for positive (sagging) moment
  • Concrete is weak in tension → we add steel bars at bottom to resist tension
  • This is the FUNDAMENTAL reason reinforced concrete works!
🔢 FORMULAS TO REMEMBER ALWAYS:

1. σ = My/I = M/Z (Flexure Formula)
2. I_rect = bh³/12 (Moment of Inertia)
3. Z_rect = bh²/6 (Section Modulus)
4. y_max = h/2 (for rectangular, distance to extreme fiber)
5. I_circle = πd⁴/64 (for circular columns)
○ Lesson 4: Shear Stresses in Beams

🎯 What You Will Learn

  • How shear stress distributes across beam depth
  • Formula for shear stress at any point
  • Why maximum shear is at neutral axis
  • Why shear matters in design (diagonal cracking!)
  • Connection to stirrup design in RC beams

📖 Theory

Shear stress is NOT uniform across beam depth! It varies parabolically - maximum at center (NA), zero at top/bottom fibers.

SHEAR STRESS FORMULA:

τ = VQ / (Ib)

Where:
τ = Shear stress at a point (MPa)
V = Shear force at section (N)
Q = First moment of area above/below the point (mm³)
I = Moment of inertia of whole section (mm⁴)
b = Width of beam at that point (mm)
FOR RECTANGULAR SECTION (MAXIMUM SHEAR):

τ_max = 3V / (2A) = 1.5 × V/A

Where:
A = Cross-sectional area = b × h

Note: Max shear is 1.5× average shear stress!
💡 Example Problem

A rectangular beam 300mm × 600mm carries a shear force of 150 kN at a section.

Find: Maximum shear stress and shear stress at 100mm from top

Solution:

A = 300 × 600 = 180,000 mm²

τ_avg = V/A = 150,000/180,000 = 0.833 MPa

τ_max = 1.5 × τ_avg = 1.5 × 0.833 = 1.25 MPa (at neutral axis)

In real RC beams, concrete alone cannot resist this - we need stirrups!

📌 THUMB RULES:
  • Shear stress is maximum at NEUTRAL AXIS (center of beam)
  • Shear stress is ZERO at extreme top and bottom fibers
  • Shear causes DIAGONAL CRACKING (at ~45° angle)
  • This is why we provide STIRRUPS (shear reinforcement) vertically
  • Near supports → High shear → More/closer stirrups needed
  • Near midspan → Low shear → Minimum stirrups OK
💡 KEY CONCEPTS:
  • Shear and bending act together - they're related (dM/dx = V)
  • Diagonal tension from shear causes cracks inclined at ~45°
  • Stirrups resist these diagonal tension forces
  • In PT slabs, shear check at column faces is CRITICAL (punching shear)
  • PT tendons help reduce shear by providing vertical component near anchors
🔢 FORMULAS TO REMEMBER ALWAYS:

1. τ = VQ/(Ib) (General shear formula)
2. τ_max(rectangular) = 3V/(2A) = 1.5V/A
3. τ_avg = V/A (Average shear stress)
4. Diagonal crack angle ≈ 45° (from principal stress theory)
📗 Module 1.2: Reinforced Concrete Design Basics 0/4
○ Lesson 1: Limit State Method & ACI 318 Introduction

🎯 What You Will Learn

  • What is Limit State Design (LSD) vs Working Stress Method (WSM)
  • Two types of limit states: Ultimate (Strength) and Serviceability
  • ACI 318 Code structure and key chapters
  • Safety factors (φ factors) - what they mean
  • Load combinations used in Dubai/UAE practice

📖 Theory

LIMIT STATE METHOD: We design structures so they don't reach "limit states" - conditions where they become unsafe or unserviceable. There are TWO types:

Type 1: ULTIMATE LIMIT STATE (ULS) - Strength
  • Structure must not collapse
  • Check: Bending failure, Shear failure, Crushing, Overturning, Buckling
  • We use FACTORED loads (increased for safety) and REDUCED capacities (φ factor)
Type 2: SERVICEABILITY LIMIT STATE (SLS)
  • Structure must remain usable during its life
  • Check: Deflection limits, Crack width control, Vibration, Durability
  • We use SERVICE (unfactored) loads
ACI 318 LOAD COMBINATIONS (Most Used in UAE):

U = 1.2D + 1.6L (Basic combination - Dead + Live)
U = 1.2D + 1.0W + 0.5L (Dead + Wind + Live)
U = 0.9D + 1.0W (Uplift/Wind uplift check)
U = 1.2D + 1.6L + 0.5(W or S) (With wind/snow)

Where:
D = Dead Load (self-weight, finishes, permanent items)
L = Live Load (people, furniture, movable items)
W = Wind Load
S = Snow Load (rarely in UAE!)
STRENGTH REDUCTION FACTORS (φ) - ACI 318 Table 21.2.1:

• φ = 0.90 → Tension-controlled flexure (beams, slabs)
• φ = 0.75 → Shear and torsion
• φ = 0.65 → Compression-controlled (columns with small eccentricity)
• φ = 0.65 → Bearing on concrete

Lower φ = More conservative (less confident in our prediction)
💡 Example: Calculate Factored Moment

Given: Service dead load moment = 120 kNm, Service live load moment = 80 kNm

Find: Factored design moment Mu

Solution:

Mu = 1.2MD + 1.6ML = 1.2(120) + 1.6(80) = 144 + 128 = 272 kNm

This Mu is what we use for designing reinforcement!

📌 THUMB RULES:
  • Always use 1.2D + 1.6L as your primary load combo for gravity design
  • φ = 0.9 for flexure (we're confident in flexural capacity predictions)
  • φ = 0.75 for shear (shear behavior is less predictable)
  • For PT design: Check BOTH transfer stage AND service stage stresses
  • UAE uses ACI 318 primarily (not Eurocode or British codes for most projects)
🔢 FORMULAS TO REMEMBER ALWAYS:

1. U = 1.2D + 1.6L (Main gravity combo)
2. Required Mn = Mu / φ (where φ=0.9 for flexure)
3. φ_flexure = 0.90
4. φ_shear = 0.75
5. φ_compression = 0.65
○ Lesson 2: Singly Reinforced Beam Design

🎯 What You Will Learn

  • Complete step-by-step beam design procedure
  • How to find required steel area (As)
  • Minimum and maximum steel ratios
  • Balanced condition and why we avoid over-reinforced beams
  • Practical design example you'll use daily

📖 Complete Design Procedure

STEP-BY-STEP BEAM DESIGN:
  1. GIVEN: Mu (factored moment), b, h, f'c, fy, cover
  2. ASSUME: Effective depth d = h - cover - stirrup - half bar dia
  3. CALCULATE: Rn = Mu / (φbd²) [Required nominal moment capacity]
  4. FIND: Steel ratio ρ from Rn equation or design charts
  5. CHECK: ρmin ≤ ρ ≤ ρmax (must be within limits!)
  6. CALCULATE: As = ρ × b × d
  7. SELECT: Number and size of bars (actual As ≥ calculated As)
  8. DESIGN: Shear reinforcement (stirrups)
KEY DESIGN EQUATIONS:

Rn = Mu / (φ b d²)

ω = fy / (0.85 f'c)

ρ = (0.85 f'c/fy) × [1 - √(1 - 2Rn/(0.85 f'c))]

Or approximate: ρ ≈ Rn / (fy × (1 - ω/2))

As_required = ρ × b × d

Mn = As × fy × (d - a/2) where a = As×fy/(0.85×f'c×b)
STEEL RATIO LIMITS (ACI 318):

ρ_min = max(200/fy, 3√f'c/fy) [for Grade 60 rebar: typically 0.0033]

ρ_max = 0.85β₁(f'c/fy) × (εu/(εu+fy/Es))

For f'c=28MPa, fy=420MPa: ρmax ≈ 0.021
For f'c=35MPa, fy=500MPa: ρmax ≈ 0.024

Rule of thumb: Keep ρ between 0.0035 and 0.02 for practical designs
💡 COMPLETE EXAMPLE - Design a Beam

GIVEN:

  • Mu = 180 kNm (factored moment)
  • b = 300mm (beam width)
  • h = 500mm (total depth)
  • f'c = 28 MPa, fy = 420 MPa
  • Cover = 40mm, Stirrups = 10mm dia

DESIGN:

Step 1: Assume #25 bars (25mm dia), effective depth:

d = 500 - 40 - 10 - 25/2 = 437.5 mm ≈ 437 mm

Step 2: Calculate Rn:

Rn = (180×10⁶) / (0.9 × 300 × 437²) = 3.47 MPa

Step 3: Find ρ (using equation or chart):

ρ ≈ 0.0087 (approximately 0.87%)

Step 4: Check limits: ρmin≈0.0033 OK, ρmax≈0.021 OK ✅

Step 5: Calculate As:

As = 0.0087 × 300 × 437 = 1141 mm²

Step 6: Select bars:

Use 4#25 (4 bars of 25mm dia) = 4×491 = 1964 mm² > 1144 ✅

(Or use 3#25 = 1473 mm² if closer match preferred)

📌 THUMB RULES FOR QUICK DESIGN:
  • For typical residential beams: ρ ≈ 0.8% to 1.5% is efficient range
  • d ≈ 0.9h (effective depth is about 90% of total depth)
  • Beam depth rule: Span/12 to Span/10 for simply supported
  • Width usually 1/3 to 1/2 of depth
  • If ρ comes out > 2%, increase beam depth (more economical than excess steel)
  • If ρ < 0.35%, use minimum steel (ρmin)
🔢 FORMULAS TO REMEMBER ALWAYS:

1. Rn = Mu/(φbd²)
2. As = ρbd
3. a = Asfy/(0.85f'cb) [compression block depth]
4. Mn = Asfy(d-a/2) [nominal moment capacity]
5. φMn ≥ Mu [design check]
○ Lesson 3: Shear Design - Stirrup Calculations

🎯 What You Will Learn

  • Concrete shear capacity (Vc) vs Steel shear capacity (Vs)
  • When stirrups are required
  • Stirrup spacing calculations
  • Minimum shear reinforcement requirements
  • Practical detailing rules

📖 Theory

Total shear resistance = Concrete contribution (Vc) + Steel contribution (Vs). If Vu > φVc, we MUST add stirrups!

SHEAR DESIGN EQUATION:

φVn ≥ Vu
where Vn = Vc + Vs

Vc = Concrete shear capacity
Vs = Steel (stirrup) shear capacity
φ = 0.75 (for shear)
Vc - CONCRETE SHEAR CAPACITY (Simplified ACI):

Vc = 0.17λ√f'c × bw × d (in SI units, result in Newtons)

OR more commonly:
Vc = (√f'c/6) × bw × d [approximate, f'c in MPa]

Where:
λ = 1.0 for normal weight concrete
bw = web width (mm)
d = effective depth (mm)
Vs - STIRRUP SHEAR CAPACITY:

Vs = Av × fy × d / s

Therefore, required spacing s:
s = Av × fy × d / Vs,required

Where:
Av = Area of stirrup legs crossing crack (mm²)
   For 2-leg stirrup: Av = 2 × (π×dia²/4)
fy = Yield strength of stirrup steel (usually 420 MPa)
s = Spacing of stirrips along beam (mm)
SPACING LIMITS (CRITICAL!):

MAXIMUM SPACING:
• s_max = d/2 (when Vs ≤ 4√f'c×bw×d)
• s_max = d/4 (when Vs > 4√f'c×bw×d)
• s_max = 300mm (absolute maximum) or 600mm in some cases

MINIMUM STEEL (when Vu < 0.5φVc, may use minimum):
Av,min = 0.062√f'c × (bw×s/fy) but not less than 0.35(bw×s/fy)
💡 Example: Design Stirrups for Beam

GIVEN:

  • Vu at critical section (d from face) = 125 kN
  • bw = 300mm, d = 450mm
  • f'c = 28 MPa, fy = 420 MPa
  • Using 10mm dia 2-leg stirrups

SOLUTION:

Step 1: Calculate Vc:

Vc = 0.17 × √28 × 300 × 450 = 0.17 × 5.29 × 300 × 450 = 121,500 N = 121.5 kN

Step 2: Check if stirrups needed:

φVc = 0.75 × 121.5 = 91.1 kN

Vu = 125 kN > 91.1 kN → YES, stirrups required!

Step 3: Calculate required Vs:

Vs,req = (Vu/φ) - Vc = (125/0.75) - 121.5 = 166.7 - 121.5 = 45.2 kN

Step 4: Calculate spacing:

Av = 2 × (π×10²/4) = 157 mm²

s = Av × fy × d / Vs,req = (157 × 420 × 450) / 45,200 = 656 mm

Step 5: Apply limits:

s_max = d/2 = 225 mm (governs) → Use s = 220 mm c/c

Provide 10mm @ 220 c/c 2-legged stirrups

📌 THUMB RULES:
  • First meter from support: Denser stirrups (high shear zone)
  • Mid-span region: Can increase spacing (lower shear)
  • Never exceed d/2 spacing when stirrups are needed
  • 10mm or 12mm stirrups most common in beams
  • Always provide minimum stirrups even if not required by calc (torsion, etc.)
  • At point of bar cutoff: Extra stirrups needed (anchorages)
🔢 FORMULAS TO REMEMBER ALWAYS:

1. Vc = 0.17√f'c × bw × d (concrete capacity)
2. Vs = Av×fy×d/s (stirrup capacity)
3. s = Av×fy×d/Vs,required (spacing calc)
4. s_max = d/2 or d/4 depending on Vs magnitude
5. φ_shear = 0.75
○ Lesson 4: One-Way Slab Design

🎯 What You Will Learn

  • When is a slab "one-way" vs "two-way"
  • Minimum slab thickness requirements
  • Design procedure using moment coefficients
  • Main reinforcement and temperature/shrinkage steel
  • Detailing rules for slabs

📖 Theory

ONE-WAY SLAB: Slab spanning mainly in ONE direction (Ly/Lx ≥ 2). Load transfer is primarily to two parallel supporting beams/walls.

MINIMUM THICKNESS (ACI 318 Table 7.3.1.1):

Simply supported one-way slab: h_min = L/20
One end continuous: h_min = L/24
Both ends continuous: h_min = L/28
Cantilever slab: h_min = L/10

Where L = clear span (mm)

These limits ensure deflection is controlled without calculation!
DESIGN MOMENT COEFFICIENTS (ACI Approximate Method):

For slabs with spans ≤ 3.6m and LL/DL ratio ≤ 3:

Positive moment (midspan):
• Simply supported: M = wL²/8
• One end continuous: M = wL²/11
• Both ends continuous: M = wL²/16

Negative moment (support):
• One end continuous: M = -wL¹⁰/14 (exterior support)
• Both ends continuous: M = -wL²/11 or -wL²/10 (interior)
REINFORCEMENT REQUIREMENTS:

MAIN REINFORCEMENT (short direction):
• Designed from moment calculation
• ρ typically 0.3% to 0.6%
• Spacing: min 3h or 300mm, max h/3 or 450mm

TEMPERATURE & SHRINKAGE REINFORCEMENT (long direction):
• Grade 40/50: As = 0.0020 × Ag (area of gross concrete)
• Grade 60: As = 0.0018 × Ag
• Spacing: max 5h or 450mm
💡 Example: Design One-Way Slab

GIVEN:

  • Clear span = 4m (simply supported)
  • Live load = 3 kPa
  • Floor finish = 1.5 kPa
  • f'c = 28 MPa, fy = 420 MPa

DESIGN:

Step 1: Thickness: h ≥ L/20 = 4000/20 = 200mm → Use h = 200mm

Step 2: Loads:

Self-weight = 0.200m × 25 kN/m³ = 5.0 kPa

Total DL = 5.0 + 1.5 = 6.5 kPa

wu = 1.2(6.5) + 1.6(3) = 7.8 + 4.8 = 12.6 kN/m (per m width)

Step 3: Moment (per 1m strip):

Mu = wuL²/8 = 12.6 × 4² / 8 = 25.2 kNm/m

Step 4: Design (d = 200-25-10 = 165mm approx):

Rn = (25.2×10⁶)/(0.9×1000×165²) = 1.03 MPa

As ≈ 260 mm²/m → Provide 10mm @ 280mm c/c (As = 280 mm²/m) ✅

Step 5: Temp/shrinkage (long dir):

As = 0.0018 × 1000 × 200 = 360 mm²/m → 10mm @ 200 c/c

📌 THUMB RULES:
  • Residential slab thickness: 150-200mm typical
  • Main steel: Usually 10mm or 12mm bars
  • Spacing: 150-250mm common for main bars
  • Temp steel: Always 10mm @ 200-250mm c/c
  • Cover: 20-25mm for slabs (exposure dependent)
  • One-way slabs span up to about 4-5m economically
🔢 FORMULAS TO REMEMBER ALWAYS:

1. h_min (one-way SS) = L/20
2. M+ (SS) = wL²/8 per m width
3. As_temp = 0.0018Ag (Grade 60)
4. Main bar spacing: max 3h or 450mm
5. Temp bar spacing: max 5h or 450mm

📕 Module 2.1: Fundamentals of Prestressed Concrete 0/4
○ Lesson 1: Introduction to Prestressing Concepts

🎯 What You Will Learn

  • What is prestressing and why we do it
  • Pre-tensioning vs Post-tensioning differences
  • Materials used in PT construction
  • Benefits of PT over conventional RC
  • Common applications in buildings

📖 Theory

PRESTRESSING: The technique of introducing internal stresses into a structural member BEFORE it carries external loads. These internal stresses are designed to COUNTERACT the stresses caused by external loading.

WHY PRESTRESS?
  • Concrete is STRONG in compression but WEAK in tension (only ~10% of compressive strength)
  • In bending, bottom fibers go into TENSION → concrete cracks
  • By PRE-COMPRESSING the tension zone, we prevent or reduce cracking
  • Result: Thinner members, longer spans, less deflection, better crack control
PRE-TENSIONING vs POST-TENSIONING:

PRE-TENSIONING:
• Tendons stressed BEFORE concrete is cast
• Tendons bonded directly to concrete
• Done in precast plant (beds required)
• Common products: Hollow core slabs, Precast beams, Railway sleepers

POST-TENSIONING (OUR FOCUS!):
• Tendons stressed AFTER concrete hardens
• Tendons in ducts, stressed against concrete member
• Done on-site (cast-in-situ)
• Common applications: Building slabs, Bridges, Water tanks, Parking structures
PT MATERIALS:

STRANDS (Tendons):
• Typically 0.5" (12.7mm) diameter 7-wire low-relaxation strand
• Ultimate strength fpu = 1860 MPa (270 ksi) - Grade 270
• Yield strength fpy ≈ 0.9 fpu = 1674 MPa
• Area of 0.5" strand = 98.7 mm² (0.153 in²)
• Modulus Ep = 195,000 MPa (28,000 ksi)

CONCRETE:
• Transfer strength f'ci ≥ 25-28 MPa (at time of stressing)
• 28-day strength f'c = 35-50 MPa typical for PT
• Higher strength allows earlier stressing

○ Lesson 2: Losses of Prestress (MOST IMPORTANT!)

⚠️ THIS IS THE #1 INTERVIEW TOPIC FOR PT JOBS!

You WILL be asked about losses. Master this completely.

📖 Complete Loss Categories

INSTANTANEOUS LOSSES (Happen immediately during stressing):
  1. Anchorage Seating (Draw-in) Loss - Wedge pulls back slightly when jack released
  2. Elastic Shortening Loss - Concrete compresses when PT force applied
  3. Friction Loss - Friction along duct during stressing
TIME-DEPENDENT LOSSES (Happen over weeks/months/years):
  1. Creep Loss - Concrete deforms under sustained compression
  2. Shrinkage Loss - Concrete volume decreases as it dries
  3. Relaxation Loss - Steel stress reduces under constant strain
LOSS 1: ANCHORAGE SEATING (DRAW-IN)

ΔP = (Ap × Ep × ΔL) / L

Where:
ΔL = Wedge draw-in (typically 6mm for wedge systems)
Ap = Area of strand(s)
Ep = 195,000 MPa
L = Length of tendon (longer tendons = less % loss!)

% Loss is HIGHER for SHORT tendons, LOWER for long tendons
LOSS 2: ELASTIC SHORTENING

Δfes = (n × fci × Ap) / Ac ≈ (Es/Ec) × fci × (Ap/Ac)

Where:
n = Modular ratio = Es/Ec ≈ 195,000/27,000 ≈ 7.2
fci = Concrete stress at centroid at transfer
Ap = Area of prestressing steel
Ac = Area of concrete section

For slabs with many tendons stressed sequentially: loss is about HALF of above
LOSS 3: FRICTION (Exponential Formula - MEMORIZE!)

Px = P0 × e^(-(Kx + μα))

Where:
Px = Force at distance x from jacking end
P0 = Jacking force at stressing end
K = Wobble friction coefficient (/meter) = 0.001-0.003/m
μ = Curvature friction coefficient = 0.05-0.30
α = Cumulative angular change (radians) from jacking end to x
x = Distance along tendon from jacking end (meters)

K accounts for unintentional misalignment of duct
μ accounts for friction against curved duct profile
LOSSES 4, 5, 6: TIME-DEPENDENT (Approximate Values)

CREEP LOSS:
Δfcr ≈ n × fci × Ψ(t,t0) × ρp
Ψ (creep coefficient) = 1.5 to 2.5 typical

SHRINKAGE LOSS:
Δfsh = εsh × Es
εsh (shrinkage strain) = 400 to 800 × 10⁻⁶ typical
So Δfsh ≈ (400-800)×10⁻⁶ × 195,000 = 78 to 156 MPa

RELAXATION LOSS:
For low-relaxation strand: ≈ 2-4% of initial stress
For stress-relieved strand: ≈ 8-12% of initial stress

Low-relaxation strand is STANDARD now - always assume this unless told otherwise
💡 COMPLETE LOSS CALCULATION EXAMPLE

GIVEN: Single span PT beam, L=12m, parabolic tendon

  • 6 strands of 0.5" dia (Ap = 6×98.7 = 592 mm²)
  • Jacking: Pi = 1300 MPa per strand (0.7 fpu)
  • f'ci = 28 MPa (transfer), f'c = 40 MPa (28-day)
  • Ec = 27,000 MPa, Es = 195,000 MPa
  • K = 0.002/m, μ = 0.20
  • ΔL (draw-in) = 6mm
  • Ac = 300,000 mm², fci at CG = 8 MPa

CALCULATE ALL LOSSES:

1. Anchorage Draw-in:

ΔP = (592 × 195,000 × 6) / 12000 = 57.72 kN (1.6% of Pi)

2. Elastic Shortening:

n = 195000/27000 = 7.22

Δfes = 7.22 × 8 × (592/300000) = 0.114 MPa (very small for this case)

3. Friction (at far end, x=12m, α≈0.16 rad for parabola):

Px = P0 × e^(-(0.002×12 + 0.20×0.16)) = P0 × e^(-0.056) = 0.946 P0

Loss = 5.4%

4. Creep (assume Ψ=2.0):

Δfcr ≈ 7.22 × 8 × 2.0 × (592/300000) = 0.228 MPa

5. Shrinkage (assume εsh=500×10⁻⁶):

Δfsh = 500×10⁻⁶ × 195000 = 97.5 MPa

6. Relaxation (low-relaxation, ~3%):

Δfrel = 0.03 × 1300 = 39 MPa

TOTAL ESTIMATED LOSSES: ~15-20%

📌 THUMB RULES FOR QUICK ESTIMATION:
  • Total losses for building slabs: typically 20-25% of Pi
  • Breakdown: Anchorage 1-3%, ES 1-3%, Friction 3-8%, Creep 4-6%, Shrinkage 5-7%, Relaxation 2-4%
  • For preliminary design: assume Pe = 0.75 to 0.80 Pi
  • Longer spans → lower % losses (friction and anchorage spread out)
  • Short tendons (< 10m) → higher % losses
  • Always use LOW-RELAXATION strand (standard nowadays)
🔢 FORMULAS TO REMEMBER ALWAYS (INTERVIEW READY!):

1. Px = P0 × e^(-(Kx+μα)) [FRICTION - #1 formula!]
2. ΔP_anchor = (Ap×Ep×ΔL)/L [Anchorage loss]
3. Total losses ≈ 20-25% of Pi for building slabs
4. Pe (effective) ≈ 0.75 to 0.80 Pi
5. K = 0.001-0.003/m, μ = 0.05-0.30
6. Wedge draw-in ΔL ≈ 6mm
○ Lesson 3: Load Balancing Method (Core Concept)

🎯 THE MOST IMPORTANT PT DESIGN CONCEPT

If you understand ONLY one thing about PT, make it this: Load Balancing.

📖 Theory

CONCEPT: A draped (curved) tendon exerts an UPWARD force on the concrete. If we make this upward force equal to the DOWNWARD gravity load, the slab sees ZERO net load → no bending stress → no deflection!

LOAD BALANCING - PARABOLIC TENDON:

Balanced Load (upward force from tendon):
Wb = (8 × P × e) / L²

Where:
P = Effective prestress force (N or kN)
e = Drape (vertical distance between high and low points) (mm or m)
L = Span length (mm or m)
Wb = Uniformly distributed upward load (N/mm or kN/m)

Units must be consistent! If P in kN, e in m, L in m → Wb in kN/m
HOW IT WORKS STEP BY STEP:

Step 1: Choose tendon profile (parabolic shape)
Step 2: Determine drape 'e' based on geometry
Step 3: Decide how much load to balance (usually 100% DL + some % of LL)
Step 4: Calculate required P using: P = Wb × L² / (8e)
Step 5: Remaining unbalanced moment = (Wtotal - Wb) × L² / 8
Step 6: Check stresses from unbalanced moment + direct compression
💡 Example: Load Balancing Calculation

GIVEN: One-way PT slab, span L = 10m

  • Slab thickness = 200mm
  • Dead load (self-weight + finishes) = 6.5 kPa
  • Live load = 3.0 kPa
  • Tendon drape e = 120mm (cover 30mm top, 30mm bottom, so e = 200-60 = 140mm, say 120mm after adjustments)
  • Effective prestress P per meter width = 400 kN/m

CALCULATION:

Step 1: Balanced load from tendon:

Wb = (8 × 400 × 0.12) / 10² = 384 / 100 = 3.84 kN/m (per m width = 3.84 kPa)

Step 2: Compare with actual loads:

DL = 6.5 kPa, so we balance 3.84/6.5 = 59% of DL

Step 3: Unbalanced load:

Wunbal = (DL + LL) - Wb = (6.5 + 3.0) - 3.84 = 5.66 kPa

Step 4: Unbalanced moment:

Munbal = 5.66 × 10² / 8 = 70.75 kNm/m

This remaining moment is designed like regular RC (add rebar if needed)

💡 KEY INSIGHTS:
  • Balance 100% of DL → slab feels weightless under dead load
  • Balance some LL too → even less deflection under live load
  • Over-balancing (>100% DL) → slab cambers upward (not good!)
  • Under-balancing → more rebar needed, more deflection
  • Optimal: Balance 80-100% DL + 20-50% LL for typical buildings
📌 THUMB RULES:
  • Typical average prestress: 1.0 to 2.0 MPa (uniform compression)
  • For 200mm slab: P ≈ 200-400 kN/m width is typical
  • Drape e ≈ slab thickness minus covers (both sides)
  • Longer spans → larger drape possible → more efficient PT
  • Short spans (< 6m) → PT may not be economical (use RC instead)
🔢 FORMULAS TO REMEMBER ALWAYS:

1. Wb = 8Pe/L² (Balanced load from parabolic tendon)
2. P = Wb×L²/(8e) (Required prestress for desired balance)
3. Unbalanced M = (Wtotal - Wb)L²/8
4. Average prestress = P/A (should be 1-2 MPa typical)
5. Eccentricity e = distance between tendon high and low points
○ Lesson 4: Tendon Profiles & Layout Patterns

🎯 What You Will Learn

  • Parabolic tendon profiles - standard shapes
  • Banded vs Distributed layout patterns
  • Where to place tendons high and low
  • Coordination with MEP penetrations
  • Practical detailing guidelines

📖 Tendon Profile Geometry

PARABOLIC PROFILE CONTROL POINTS:
  • High Points (near supports/columns): Tendon near TOP of slab (resists negative/hogging moment)
  • Low Point (midspan): Tendon near BOTTOM of slab (resists positive/sagging moment)
  • Shape: Parabolic curve between control points
  • Transition Point: Inflection point where curvature reverses (point of contraflexure)
TYPICAL COVER REQUIREMENTS:

Top cover (at high point): 30-40mm to CG of strand
Bottom cover (at low point): 30-40mm to CG of strand

For 200mm slab with 40mm cover each side:
Max available drape e = 200 - 40 - 40 = 120mm
Actual drape may be 100-110mm (accounting for strand dia, tolerances)
LAYOUT PATTERNS (Two-Way Systems):

PATTERN A: BANDED-DISTRIBUTED (Most Common in UAE)
• Direction 1: Tendons BANDED over column lines (like hidden beams)
• Direction 2: Tendons DISTRIBUTED uniformly across width
• Advantages: Easy MEP coordination, clear load path
• Disadvantage: Some inefficiency in distributed direction

PATTERN B: UNIFORM BOTH WAYS
• Both directions: Uniformly spaced tendons
• Advantage: Most theoretically optimal for square panels
• Disadvantage: Harder to coordinate MEP conflicts

PATTERN C: ALL BANDED
• Both directions banded (forms grid over columns)
• Rarely used - mostly special cases
💡 Practical Example - Tendon Layout Decision

Panel: 8m × 8m square, flat plate, 200mm thick

Decision Process:

  1. Panel is square (aspect ratio 1:1) → both directions equally important
  2. Choose Banded-Distributed pattern (industry standard)
  3. Direction X (say North-South): Band tendons over columns
  4. Direction Y (East-West): Distribute uniformly
  5. Band width: typically 1.2-1.5m wide over column
  6. Spaced tendons: typically 800-1000mm c/c in distributed direction
📌 THUMB RULES:
  • Standard tendon spacing: 800-1200mm c/c typical
  • Band width over columns: 1.0-1.5m typical
  • Keep tendons at least 150mm away from slab edges
  • Never cut a tendon for MEP - relocate tendon or penetration
  • Large openings (>300mm): Add extra tendons around opening perimeter
  • Minimum 2 tendons through column strip in each direction
🔢 FORMULAS TO REMEMBER ALWAYS:

1. Available drape e = h - cover_top - cover_bottom
2. Parabolic profile: y = 4e(x)(L-x)/L² (equation of parabola)
3. Transition point (inflection): typically at L/5 to L/6 from support
4. Tendon spacing: 800-1200mm c/c typical

○ Lesson 1: Stress Checks at Transfer & Service Stage

🎯 What You Will Learn

  • Transfer stage (initial) stress checks
  • Service stage (long-term) stress checks
  • Stress calculation methodology

📖 ACI 318 Stress Limits (Chapter 18)

STRESS LIMITS AT TRANSFER (Immediately after stressing):

Compressive stress (extreme fiber): ≤ 0.60f'ci
Tensile stress (extreme fiber, no bonded rebar): ≤ 0.25√f'ci
Tensile stress (with sufficient bonded rebar): Can allow higher (but cracks expected)

Where f'ci = Concrete strength at transfer (typically 25-28 MPa)
STRESS LIMITS AT SERVICE (After all losses, long-term):

Compressive stress: ≤ 0.45f'c
Tensile stress: Decompression allowed (f't = 0 permitted) with proper bonded rebar
For Class U (uncracked): f't ≤ 0.5√f'c
For Class T (transitional): f't ≤ 7.5√f'c
For Class C (cracked): No explicit limit, but cracked section analysis required

Building slabs are usually designed as Class U or T
STRESS CALCULATION FORMULA:

f = P/A ± Pe(y)/I ± M(y)/I

Or simplified for rectangular sections:
f_top_or_bottom = P/A ± M/S ± Pe/S

Where:
P = Prestress force (Pe for service, Pi for transfer)
e = Eccentricity at section considered
A = Area of cross-section
I = Moment of inertia
y = Distance from NA to fiber being checked
S = Section modulus = I/y
M = Moment from external loads (including unbalanced PT effects)
💡 Example: Stress Check at Midspan

GIVEN: One-way slab strip 1m wide, h=200mm

  • P = 350 kN/m (effective after losses)
  • e at midspan = 85mm (toward bottom)
  • M_service (DL+LL) = 45 kNm/m
  • f'c = 35 MPa

Check service stage stresses:

A = 1000 × 200 = 200,000 mm²

I = 1000 × 200³ / 12 = 666.7 × 10⁶ mm⁴

S_top = S_bottom = I/(h/2) = 666.7×10⁶/100 = 6.67×10⁶ mm³

Direct stress: P/A = 350,000/200,000 = 1.75 MPa (compression throughout)

Bending stress from eccentricity: Pe/S = 350,000×85/6.67×10⁶ = 4.46 MPa

   → Bottom: +4.46 MPa (more compression) = 1.75+4.46 = 6.21 MPa (C)

   → Top: -4.46 MPa (less compression) = 1.75-4.46 = -2.71 MPa (T!)

External moment stress: M/S = 45×10⁶/6.67×10⁶ = 6.75 MPa

   → Bottom: -6.75 MPa (tension from load)

   → Top: +6.75 MPa (compression from load)

TOTAL STRESS:

Bottom: 6.21 - 6.75 = -0.54 MPa (slight tension) ← Check limit: 0.5√35 = 2.96 MPa ✅

Top: -2.71 + 6.75 = +4.04 MPa (compression) ← Check limit: 0.45×35 = 15.75 MPa ✅

📌 THUMB RULES:
  • Top fiber at midspan: Usually in tension at transfer (OK if within limit)
  • Bottom fiber at support: Usually in tension at transfer (due to high eccentricity)
  • Add bonded rebar if tensile stress exceeds allowable
  • Typical service stress: 2-5 MPa compression at highly stressed locations
  • If compression > 0.45f'c: Increase slab thickness or reduce prestress
🔢 FORMULAS TO REMEMBER ALWAYS:

1. Transfer comp. limit: 0.60f'ci
2. Transfer tens. limit: 0.25√f'ci (no bonded rebar)
3. Service comp. limit: 0.45f'c
4. Service tens. limit: 0.5√f'c (Class U)
5. f = P/A ± Pe/S ± M/S (combined stress equation)
○ Lesson 2: Punching Shear Check (Critical!)

⚠️ MOST COMMON FAILURE MODE IN FLAT SLABS!

Punching shear is the #1 thing you check in every PT flat slab design. Columns "punch" through the slab.

📖 Theory

PUNCHING SHEAR: A concentrated shear failure around column heads in flat plates/slabs. The column appears to "punch" through the slab along a truncated cone/pyramid surface.

CRITICAL SECTION LOCATION (ACI 318):

At distance d/2 from face of column (on all sides)

Where d = effective depth of slab

For interior column b₀ (perimeter of critical section):
• Square column c×c: b₀ = 2(c+d) + 2(c+d) = 4(c+d)
• Rectangular column: b₀ = 2(c₁+d) + 2(c₂+d)
• Circular column dia D: b₀ = π(D+d)
PUNCHING SHEAR CAPACITY (ACI 318 Eq. 22.6.5.2):

φVc = φ × Vc

For non-PT slabs, Vc is the smallest of:
1. Vc = (1 + 2/β) × λ√f'c × b₀ × d / (usual factor)
2. Vc = (αsd/d + 2) × λ√f'c × b₀ × d
3. Vc = (limit) × λ√f'c × b₀ × d

Simplified approach (conservative):
Vc ≈ 0.33√f'c × b₀ × d (for interior columns, no PT effect)

φ = 0.75 (for shear)
PT CONTRIBUTION TO PUNCHING SHEAR:

Vertical component of PT force within d/2 zone helps!

Vp = (applies vertical upward force reducing net Vu)

Net shear: Vu_net = Vu - Vp

Also, PT compression increases Vc slightly (ACI allows increase factor)
💡 Example: Punching Shear Check

GIVEN: Interior column 500mm×500mm, PT flat plate 200mm thick

  • Factored shear from column Vu = 650 kN
  • f'c = 35 MPa, d = 170mm (to CG of bonded steel)
  • No shear reinforcement (initially)

CHECK:

Step 1: Critical perimeter b₀:

b₀ = 4 × (500 + 170) = 4 × 670 = 2680 mm

Step 2: Punching shear capacity (simplified):

Vc = 0.33 × √35 × 2680 × 170 = 0.33 × 5.92 × 2680 × 170 = 887,000 N = 887 kN

Step 3: Apply φ factor:

φVc = 0.75 × 887 = 665 kN

Step 4: Compare:

Vu = 650 kN ≤ φVc = 665 kN → OK! No shear reinforcement needed ✅

If Vu > φVc: Need stud rails, drop panels, or column capitals!

📌 WHAT TO DO IF PUNCHING SHEAR FAILS:
  • Option 1: Increase slab thickness (most effective but costly)
  • Option 2: Add drop panel (thickened slab around column)
  • Option 3: Add column capital (flared column head)
  • Option 4: Add shear studs/stirrups (most common solution!)
  • Option 5: Increase column size (larger b₀)
  • Stud rail systems (like Decon, Nelson Studs) are very common in Dubai PT buildings
🔢 FORMULAS TO REMEMBER ALWAYS:

1. Critical section at d/2 from column face
2. b₀ (square col) = 4(c + d)
3. Vc ≈ 0.33√f'c × b₀ × d (approximate)
4. φVc ≥ Vu (must satisfy!)
5. φ_shear = 0.75
○ Lesson 3: Deflection Control in PT Slabs

🎯 What You Will Learn

  • Why PT slabs deflect less than RC slabs
  • Immediate vs long-term deflection components
  • ACI 318 deflection limits
  • Calculation methods (immediate and time-dependent)
  • Camber considerations

📖 Theory

WHY PT DEFLECTS LESS: The balanced load from tendons counteracts gravity deflection. Only UNBALANCED load causes deflection - which is much smaller than total load.

DEFLECTION COMPONENTS:

TOTAL DEFLECTION = Immediate + Time-dependent

IMMEDIATE (when load applied):
• From unbalanced loads: δ_inst = 5(Wunbal)L⁴/(384EcI) for simple span
• From PT (upward camber): δ_pt = 5(Wb)L⁴/(384EcI) (upward!)

LONG-TERM (creep + shrinkage over years):
• δ_LT = δ_inst × λ_factor
• λ = ξ / (1 + 50ρ') where ξ = 2.0 (5 years), 1.4 (1yr), etc.
ACI 318 TABLE 24.2.2 - DEFLECTION LIMITS:

Flat roofs not supporting elements: L/180
Floors not supporting elements: L/360
Roofs or floors supporting brittle elements: L/480

For L = 8m: Floor limit = 8000/360 = 22mm
💡 Quick Deflection Estimate

GIVEN: PT one-way slab, L=8m, h=200mm

  • W_total = 10 kPa, W_balanced = 7 kPa
  • W_unbalanced = 3 kPa
  • Ec = 28,000 MPa, Ig = 1000×200³/12 = 667×10⁶ mm⁴

Immediate deflection from unbalanced load:

δ = 5 × (3 N/mm) × (8000mm)⁴ / (384 × 28000 × 667×10⁶)

δ = 5 × 3 × 4.096×10¹⁵ / (7.174×10¹³) = 0.86 mm

Compare to limit: L/360 = 22mm → 0.86 << 22 ✅ EXCELLENT!

This is why PT slabs perform so well - tiny deflections!

📌 THUMB RULES:
  • PT slab deflection is typically 1/3 to 1/5 of equivalent RC slab
  • If immediate deflection > L/180: redesign (increase thickness or prestress)
  • Long-term deflection ≈ 2-3 × immediate deflection
  • Camber (upward bow) from PT is normal - don't over-balance!
  • Check deflection at BOTH transfer and service stages
  • For preliminary design: if span/depth ≤ 40, deflection usually OK for PT
🔢 FORMULAS TO REMEMBER ALWAYS:

1. Floor deflection limit: L/360
2. Roof limit: L/180
3. δ = 5WL⁴/(384EI) (simple span UDL)
4. Long-term multiplier: λ ≈ 2-3 for typical cases
5. Span/depth for PT: L/40 to L/45 typical (vs L/24 to L/28 for RC)

○ Lesson 1: Short Column Design (Axial + Moment)

🎯 What You Will Learn

  • Interaction diagram (P-M relationship)
  • Design of tied columns

📖 Key Concepts

SHORT COLUMN: Slenderness ratio kl/r ≤ 22 (or approximately kl ≤ 10-12×depth). Failure is by material crushing, not buckling.

NOMINAL AXIAL CAPACITY (ACI 318 Eq. 22.4.2):

Pn = 0.85fc'(Ag - As) + fy As

φPn = φ[0.85fc'(Ag - As) + fy As]

Where:
φ = 0.65 (for tied columns)
Ag = Gross area of column (mm²)
As = Total longitudinal steel area (mm²)
fc' = Concrete compressive strength (MPa)
fy = Steel yield strength (MPa)
REINFORCEMENT LIMITS (ACI 318):

ρ_min = 0.01 (1% of Ag)
ρ_max = 0.08 (8% of Ag) - but practically 0.04 (4%)

Typical range: ρ = 0.01 to 0.03 (1% to 3%)

Minimum bars: 4 for tied rect., 6 for circular
Min bar size: #5 (16mm) for tied columns
TIE REQUIREMENTS (ACI 318 25.7.2):

Tie size: ≥ #3 (#10) for #10 and smaller longitudinal bars
             ≥ #4 (#13) for #11, #14, #18 and bundled bars
Spacing: ≤ 16db (longitudinal bar dia)
          ≤ 48db (tie dia)
          ≤ least column dimension
Configuration: Every corner bar + alternate bar supported
💡 Example: Design Short Column

GIVEN: Pu = 3000 kN, Mu = 80 kNm, column 500×500mm

f'c = 40 MPa, fy = 500 MPa

Check axial capacity first (ignore moment initially):

Ag = 500 × 500 = 250,000 mm²

Assume ρ = 1.5% → As = 0.015 × 250,000 = 3750 mm²

Pn = 0.85×40×(250000-3750) + 500×3750 = 8,347,500 + 1,875,000 = 10,222,500 N

φPn = 0.65 × 10,222.5 kN = 6,644 kN >> Pu = 3000 kN ✅

Select reinforcement: Use 6#25 (6×491 = 2946 mm², ρ=1.18%)

Full interaction diagram analysis needed for combined P-M (done via software or charts)

📌 THUMB RULES:
  • Typical column sizes: 300-600mm square for residential
  • Typical reinforcement: 1-2.5% steel ratio
  • #20 to #32 bars typical for main reinforcement
  • #10 or #12 ties typical
  • Tie spacing: usually 150-200mm c/c
  • Lap splices: 40-50db in middle height of column
🔢 FORMULAS TO REMEMBER ALWAYS:

1. Pn = 0.85fc'(Ag-As) + fyAs (axial capacity)
2. φ_column = 0.65 (tied), 0.75 (spiral)
3. ρ_min = 0.01, ρ_max_practical = 0.04
4. Tie spacing: min(16db_long, 48db_tie, col_dim)
5. Short column: kl/r ≤ 22
○ Lesson 2: Foundation Design (Isolated Footing)

🎯 What You Will Learn

  • Sizing footings for bearing pressure
  • One-way shear check (beam shear)
  • Two-way shear check (punching shear)
  • Flexural reinforcement design
  • Development length and detailing

📖 Design Procedure

ISOLATED FOOTING DESIGN STEPS:
  1. Determine footing size: Based on SBC (safe bearing capacity)
  2. Calculate net upward pressure: qu = Pu/Area - self-weight correction
  3. Check one-way shear: At d from column face
  4. Check punching shear: At d/2 from column face
  5. Design flexural reinforcement: At face of column
  6. Check development length: Bars must develop full strength
  7. Detail: Cover, minimum steel, dowels to column
FOOTING SIZE FROM BEARING:

Area_required = Pu / (0.5 × SBC) [factor 0.5 for rough estimate including self-weight]

Or more precisely:
Area = (Pu + 1.2DL_footing) / SBC_allowable

For square footing: B = √Area

Where SBC = Safe Bearing Capacity (kPa)
ONE-WAY SHEAR (BEAM SHEAR) CHECK:

Critical section: At distance d from column face
Vu_one-way = qu × B × ((B-c)/2 - d)

φVc = φ × 0.17√f'c × B × d
Require: φVc ≥ Vu_one-way
PUNCHING SHEAR (TWO-WAY) CHECK:

Critical section: At d/2 from column face (perimeter b₀)
Vu_punch = Pu - qu × (c+d)² [subtract pressure inside critical area]

φVc = φ × 0.33√f'c × b₀ × d
Require: φVc ≥ Vu_punch
